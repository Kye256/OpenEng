### M.1.2 Institutional Context

**Kenya Road Authorities:** KeNHA (Kenya National Highways Authority) manages national and international trunk roads. KeRRA (Kenya Rural Roads Authority) manages rural and small town roads. KURA (Kenya Urban Roads Authority) manages urban roads. County Governments manage county roads. The Ministry of Roads and Transport (MoRT) provides policy oversight.

**No published Kenya rate database:** Kenya does not have a publicly available unit rate database equivalent to Uganda's Skill #53 (Uganda/EA Unit Rate Database). Rate build-up is from first principles using project-specific inputs, cross-checked against recent contract award prices where available. KeNHA maintains internal records of contract awards but these are not publicly available.

**NCA contractor registration:** The National Construction Authority (NCA) registers contractors in categories (NCA 1 through NCA 8, with NCA 1 being the highest). Contractor category determines the maximum contract value the contractor can undertake. This affects rate analysis because smaller contractors (NCA 6-8) typically have higher overhead percentages relative to turnover.

### M.1.3 Standard Philosophy

Rate build-up in Kenya follows the universal L+E+M+OH methodology. Kenya-specific factors include: moderate labour costs relative to East Africa, significant transport costs for materials in remote areas, imported bitumen (through Mombasa port, USD-linked), strong domestic cement industry, and significant regional cost variation.

## M.3 Country-Specific Reasoning

### M.3.1 Decision Logic (Country Branch)

**Kenya-Specific Cost Factors:**

1. **Transport dominates remote area rates:** For projects in North Eastern and Upper Eastern Kenya, transport cost of aggregates and gravel from source to site often exceeds the material cost. Always calculate transport explicitly using the tonne-km rate.

2. **Imported materials:** Bitumen is imported through Mombasa port. Steel is both imported and domestically produced. Prices are partly USD-linked and subject to exchange rate fluctuation. For rate analysis, note the exchange rate assumption.

3. **Domestic cement advantage:** Kenya has a strong domestic cement industry (Bamburi, East African Portland, Mombasa Cement, National Cement, Savannah Cement, and others). This results in relatively stable and competitive cement prices compared to countries that import cement.

4. **Equipment availability:** Specialised equipment (asphalt plants, large cranes, piling rigs) is concentrated in the Nairobi-Mombasa-Kisumu corridor. Mobilisation cost to remote sites (North Eastern, Northern Rift) can be significant.

5. **Overhead and profit percentages (ILLUSTRATIVE):**

| Component | Range | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Site overheads | 12 - 18% | Depends on project scale and remoteness |
| Head office overheads | 6 - 10% | Depends on contractor size |
| Profit | 8 - 15% | Depends on competition level and risk |

ILLUSTRATIVE -- actual percentages depend on contractor and market conditions.

### M.3.2 Professional Judgment (Country-Specific)

- **Check rates against recent KeNHA awards:** Rates built up from first principles should be cross-checked against actual contract award prices where available.
- **Fuel price sensitivity:** Equipment rates are sensitive to diesel price. The Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA) sets maximum fuel prices monthly -- these change frequently.
- **Quarry royalties and NEMA requirements:** Quarry and borrow pit exploitation requires NEMA approval and payment of royalties to county governments. These costs must be included in material rates.

### M.3.3 Common Errors (Country-Specific)

1. **Underestimating gravel haul distance:** In North Eastern Kenya, suitable borrow areas may be 30-50 km from the project site. Average haul distances of 15-25 km are common.
2. **Using Nairobi equipment rates for remote projects:** Equipment hire rates in Nairobi are lower due to competition. Remote area rates include mobilisation premium.
3. **Ignoring altitude effects on equipment productivity:** Kenya Highlands projects (>2,000m) experience reduced engine output. Account for derating in production rates.
4. **Not including NEMA/county environmental costs:** Quarry and borrow pit exploitation requires environmental assessment and county government permits. Include these costs in material rates.

## M.7 Worked Example -- Kenya

### Example: Build Up Unit Rate for Gravel Base Course (Series 4000) -- Western Kenya

**Source:** Illustrative rate build-up. ALL RATES ARE PLACEHOLDER.

**Given:**
- Item: Graded crushed stone base course, 200mm compacted thickness (per Section 4300 of Standard Specification)
- Method: Mechanical (loader, trucks, motor grader, vibratory roller, water bowser)
- Location: Western Kenya (Kisumu area, regional factor 1.15)
- Quarry distance: 15 km from site
- Production rate: Motor grader spreading, roller compaction, 4 passes = 150 m3/day compacted output for a typical spread

**Rate Build-Up:**

**LABOUR**

| Category | Hours/m3 | Rate (KES/hr) | Cost (KES/m3) |
|----------|---------|---------------|---------------|
| Grader operator | 0.053 | 500 | 27 |
| Roller operator | 0.053 | 500 | 27 |
| Water bowser driver | 0.053 | 375 | 20 |
| Labourers (4 no.) | 0.213 | 125 | 27 |
| **Labour subtotal** | | | **101** |

Note: 0.053 hr/m3 = 8 hrs / 150 m3/day

**EQUIPMENT**

| Type | Hours/m3 | Rate (KES/hr) | Cost (KES/m3) |
|------|---------|---------------|---------------|
| Motor grader | 0.053 | 14,000 | 742 |
| Vibratory roller (10t) | 0.053 | 8,000 | 424 |
| Water bowser (10,000L) | 0.053 | 6,000 | 318 |
| **Equipment subtotal** | | | **1,484** |

**MATERIALS**

| Material | Qty/m3 | Cost/unit (KES) | Wastage | Cost (KES/m3) |
|----------|--------|-----------------|---------|---------------|
| Crushed stone aggregate (ex-quarry) | 1.25 m3 loose | 3,500/m3 | 5% | 4,594 |
| Transport (15 km, 1.6 t/m3 loose) | 1.25 x 1.6 x 15 | 12/t-km | -- | 360 |
| Water for compaction | 50 litres | 0.5/litre | -- | 25 |
| **Materials subtotal** | | | | **4,979** |

Note: 1.25 m3 loose per 1.0 m3 compacted (bulking factor). Wastage 5% applied to aggregate only.

**DIRECT COST:** KES 101 + 1,484 + 4,979 = **KES 6,564/m3**

**REGIONAL ADJUSTMENT:** Western Kenya factor 1.15
- Adjusted direct cost: KES 6,564 x 1.15 = **KES 7,549/m3**

**OVERHEADS AND PROFIT**

| Item | Percentage | Amount (KES/m3) |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| Direct cost (adjusted) | -- | 7,549 |
| Site overheads | 15% | 1,132 |
| Subtotal | -- | 8,681 |
| Head office overheads | 8% | 695 |
| Subtotal | -- | 9,376 |
| Profit | 10% | 938 |
| **Total unit rate** | -- | **10,314** |

**PLACEHOLDER RATE: KES 10,314/m3** for graded crushed stone base course (200mm compacted) in Western Kenya.

**Cross-check:** Range check KES 4,000-10,000/m3 for crushed stone base (PLACEHOLDER range). Result is at the upper end due to the 15 km transport distance and Western Kenya regional factor. For a project closer to the quarry or in the Central region, the rate would be lower.
