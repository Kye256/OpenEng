### M.1.2 Institutional Context

**Kenya Road Authorities:** KeNHA (Kenya National Highways Authority) manages national and international trunk roads. KeRRA (Kenya Rural Roads Authority) manages rural and small town roads. KURA (Kenya Urban Roads Authority) manages urban roads. County Governments manage county roads. The Ministry of Roads and Transport (MoRT) provides policy oversight.

**PPRA Context:** The Public Procurement and Asset Disposal Act 2015 establishes procurement method thresholds based on estimated contract value. The engineer's estimate determines which procurement method applies -- open tendering, restricted tendering, request for quotations, or direct procurement. The Accounting Officer or Procuring Entity uses the engineer's estimate as the benchmark for bid evaluation and budget allocation.

**Procuring entities for road projects:** KeNHA, KeRRA, KURA, and County Governments each act as procuring entities for their respective road networks. For donor-funded projects (World Bank, AfDB, JICA, EU), procurement follows donor rules with PPRA as the fallback national framework.

**Engineer's estimate role:** The estimate determines procurement method under PPRA, establishes the budget baseline for the Accounting Officer, and provides the benchmark against which tendered prices are evaluated. Cross-reference to Skill #47 Kenya module for PPRA threshold implications.

### M.1.3 Standard Philosophy

Kenya does not have a published standard cost estimation manual. Practice follows international cost engineering norms applied to Kenyan conditions. The WSCM Part 3 BoQ provides the standardised format for cost build-up. KeNHA maintains records of recent contract award prices for benchmarking, but these are not publicly available as a rate database. Kenya does not have an equivalent to Uganda's Skill #53 (Uganda/EA Unit Rate Database).

## M.2 Country-Specific Knowledge

### M.2.1 Kenya Cost Estimation Approach

**(a) WSCM Part 3 BoQ as basis for estimate:** The engineer's estimate is built from quantities derived from design outputs (upstream technical skills) priced using the WSCM Part 3 BoQ format. The BoQ series structure aligns with the Standard Specification (WSCM Part 2):

- Series 1000: General (Preliminaries, Site Establishment, Traffic Accommodation, Overhaul, Testing, Quality Control)
- Series 2000: Drainage
- Series 3000: Earthworks
- Series 4000: Natural, Crushed Stone and Stabilised Pavement Layers
- Series 5000: Bituminous Surface Treatments, Seals and Pavement Layers
- Series 6000: Concrete Pavements and Modular Paving
- Series 7000: Structures
- Series 8000: Ancillary Roadworks (Markers, Guardrails, Fencing, Signs, Markings, Landscaping)
- Series 9000: Environmental, Health, Safety and Social Aspects

## M.3 Country-Specific Reasoning

### M.3.1 Decision Logic (Country Branch)

**Kenya Cost Estimation Context:**

1. **Currency:** KES (Kenya Shillings) is the primary currency. For donor-funded projects, the estimate includes a foreign currency component (typically USD) for imported items: bitumen, steel, specialist equipment parts. A typical split is 65-75% local (KES) and 25-35% foreign (USD), depending on project scope and import content.

2. **VAT:** 16% VAT is the standard rate in Kenya (as at time of writing -- VERIFY CURRENT RATE). Some donor-funded projects have VAT exemption agreements. If VAT-exempt, the estimate should show "VAT: Exempt per [agreement]."

3. **PPRA threshold implications:** The engineer's estimate value determines the procurement method. PPRA thresholds are periodically updated -- all values below are ILLUSTRATIVE and must be verified against current PPRA regulations:
   - Open tendering: Above specified threshold (default method)
   - Restricted tendering: Permitted under specific conditions per Section 90 PPRA
   - Request for quotations: Below specified threshold
   - Direct procurement: Emergency or sole source situations per Section 103 PPRA
   
   The estimate must be prepared before procurement method selection.

4. **Professional fees:** Supervision consultant fees are typically 8-12% of construction cost for Kenya road projects. Design consultant fees are typically 5-8%. These are separate from the construction estimate but relevant for total project cost.

### M.3.2 Professional Judgment (Country-Specific)

- **Remote area mobilisation costs:** North Eastern Kenya projects (Garissa, Mandera, Wajir) have significantly higher mobilisation costs due to distance from Nairobi, poor access roads, limited local accommodation, and security requirements. Mobilisation can be 5-8% of contract value compared to 3-5% in accessible areas.
- **County road access fees:** Some County Governments charge access fees or require permits for use of county roads during construction. Include provision in the estimate if applicable.
- **Material availability:** Kenya has good domestic cement production (multiple manufacturers) and aggregate supply in central/western regions. Bitumen is imported through Mombasa. Northern and eastern regions have limited local material sources -- longer haul distances significantly increase costs.
- **Seasonal pricing:** Bitumen and fuel prices are linked to global crude oil prices and KES/USD exchange rate. Cement prices are more stable due to domestic production. Aggregate costs depend on quarry proximity.

### M.3.3 Common Errors (Country-Specific)

1. **Underestimating remote area mobilisation:** North Eastern Kenya projects require significant mobilisation provisions. Failing to account for camp establishment, water supply, and access road maintenance leads to underestimates.
2. **Not accounting for county road access and permits:** Some county roads used for construction access may require permits or restoration works. Include as provisional sums.
3. **Using Nairobi-region rates for upcountry projects:** Regional cost variation can be 25-45% above Nairobi baseline for remote areas. Always apply appropriate regional adjustment factors.
4. **Ignoring exchange rate sensitivity:** For projects with significant import content (bitumen, steel, equipment), exchange rate movements between KES and USD can significantly affect the estimate.

## M.7 Worked Example -- Kenya

### Example: Engineer's Estimate for 25 km Class C Road Rehabilitation in Western Kenya

**Source:** Illustrative example. ALL AMOUNTS ARE PLACEHOLDER.

**Given:**
- 25 km Class C road rehabilitation, Kisumu-Kericho corridor (Western Kenya)
- BoQ from Skill #51 worked example (WSCM Part 3 format)
- PLACEHOLDER rates
- Project stage: Detailed design
- Contract duration: 18 months
- Inflation: 6% per annum (illustrative)
- VAT: 16%
- Exchange rate: KES 150 = USD 1 (illustrative)
- Expenditure profile: Year 1 = 60%, Year 2 (6 months) = 40%
- Regional adjustment: Western Kenya factor 1.15

**Solution:**

**Step 1: Series Subtotals (PLACEHOLDER)**

| Series | Description | Subtotal (KES, millions) | Subtotal (USD, thousands) |
|--------|-------------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| 1000 | General (Preliminaries, Establishment, Traffic, Testing) | 200 | 1,333 |
| 2000 | Drainage (ditches, culverts, erosion protection) | 250 | 1,667 |
| 3000 | Earthworks (patching, formation repair) | 150 | 1,000 |
| 4000 | Pavement Layers (base repair, sub-base) | 400 | 2,667 |
| 5000 | Bituminous (prime, tack, AC overlay) | 600 | 4,000 |
| 6000 | Concrete Pavements | 0 | 0 |
| 7000 | Structures (minor culvert repairs) | 100 | 667 |
| 8000 | Ancillary (signs, markings, guardrails) | 80 | 533 |
| 9000 | Environmental, Health, Safety | 50 | 333 |
| | **Total Direct Cost** | **1,830** | **12,200** |

Note: Series subtotals already include Western Kenya regional adjustment factor of 1.15.

**Step 2: Physical Contingency**
- Stage: Detailed design -> 10%
- Physical contingency = KES 1,830M x 10% = KES 183M (USD 1,220K)

**Step 3: Price Contingency**
- Contract duration: 18 months, inflation: 6%/year
- Year 1 expenditure (60%): KES 1,098M x 0% = KES 0M (base year)
- Year 2 expenditure (40%): KES 732M x 6% = KES 43.9M
- Total price contingency = KES 43.9M (USD 293K)

**Step 4: Subtotal before VAT**
- KES 1,830M + 183M + 43.9M = KES 2,056.9M (USD 13,713K)

**Step 5: VAT**
- VAT = KES 2,056.9M x 16% = KES 329.1M (USD 2,194K)

**Step 6: Total Estimate**
- Total = KES 2,056.9M + 329.1M = **KES 2,386M** (**USD 15,907K**)

**Step 7: Cost per km**
- KES 2,386M / 25 km = KES 95.4M/km (USD 636K/km)
- Benchmark: KES 40-120M/km for rehabilitation in Kenya (PLACEHOLDER)
- Result is within the benchmark range

**Step 8: PPRA Threshold Check**
- Total estimate KES 2,386M -- exceeds open tendering threshold
- Procurement method: Open tendering (National or International per PPRA)

**ALL AMOUNTS ARE PLACEHOLDER -- FOR METHODOLOGY DEMONSTRATION ONLY**

**Verification:** Cost per km within the benchmark range. Physical contingency at 10% appropriate for detailed design. Price contingency calculation uses compound approach. Western Kenya regional factor of 1.15 applied to direct costs.
