### M.1.2 Institutional Context

**KeNHA (Kenya National Highways Authority):** Manages maintenance of national trunk roads (Class A/B/C). Prepares annual maintenance work plans based on condition data from network surveys. Maintenance budget from Road Maintenance Levy Fund (RMLF) allocation via KRB. KeNHA uses both force account and contracted maintenance, including Output and Performance-based Road Contracts (OPRC/PBMC) for major corridor maintenance.

**KeRRA (Kenya Rural Roads Authority):** Manages maintenance of rural roads (Class D/E). Maintenance is predominantly routine (grading, drain clearing) with periodic gravel resheeting. Limited capacity for paved road periodic maintenance. Annual maintenance planning uses visual condition data.

**KURA (Kenya Urban Roads Authority):** Manages urban road maintenance. Urban maintenance includes pavement repair plus utility coordination, drainage, pedestrian facilities, and traffic management during works. Higher unit costs due to urban working conditions.

**County Governments:** Manage maintenance of devolved county roads. Maintenance capacity varies significantly -- some counties have dedicated road units, others contract all work. County road maintenance budgets are typically constrained.

**Kenya Roads Board (KRB):** Administers the Road Maintenance Levy Fund (RMLF) derived from the fuel levy. KRB allocates RMLF to KeNHA, KeRRA, KURA, and counties using a formula based on road network length, condition, and usage. KRB also coordinates maintenance standards and reporting. The fuel levy is the primary ring-fenced maintenance funding source in Kenya.

### M.1.3 Standard Philosophy

Kenya maintenance planning per RDM 5.2 follows a condition-responsive approach: condition data from RDM 5.1 surveys determines intervention triggers, and RDM 5.2 Chapter 7 provides criteria for selecting between maintenance, rehabilitation, and reconstruction. The approach distinguishes between surface condition criteria (IRI, PCI, cracking, rutting) and structural criteria (deflections). RDM 5.2 includes dedicated chapters for unpaved roads (Ch 14), urban roads (Ch 13), and climate resilience considerations (Ch 12). Performance-based maintenance contracts (OPRC model) are increasingly used by KeNHA for major corridors.

## M.2 Country-Specific Knowledge

### M.2.1 Kenya Maintenance Intervention Criteria

**Surface Condition Criteria (RDM 5.2 Table 7.1):**

Surface condition triggers are based on cracking index, rutting depth, and IRI:

| Parameter | Routine Maintenance | Periodic Maintenance | Rehabilitation | Unit |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|---------------|------|
| PCI (RDM 5.1) | > 60 | 25 - 60 | < 25 | % |
| IRI (paved) | < 4 | 4 - 6 | > 6 | m/km |
| Rut depth | < 10 | 10 - 20 | > 20 | mm |
| Cracking extent | < 10 | 10 - 50 | > 50 | % area |

**Present Serviceability Rating (RDM 5.2 Table 7.2):**

| PSR | Condition | Intervention |
|-----|-----------|-------------|
| 4-5 | Good to Very Good | Routine maintenance |
| 2-3 | Fair | Periodic maintenance or rehabilitation |
| 0-1 | Poor to Very Poor | Rehabilitation or reconstruction |

**Deflection Criteria (RDM 5.2 Section 7.3):**

Structural adequacy is assessed using FWD deflections. When tolerable deflection is exceeded, the pavement has insufficient structural capacity for its design traffic. RDM 5.2 provides Kenya-specific characteristic/critical deflection curves for flexible pavements (Figure 7.2).

### M.2.2 Maintenance Treatment Practice in Kenya

**Routine Maintenance Activities:**
- Pothole patching (cold mix or hot mix depending on availability and road class)
- Crack sealing (bitumen or elastomeric sealant)
- Drainage clearing (side drains, mitre drains, culvert inlets/outlets)
- Vegetation control (grass cutting, bush clearing within road reserve)
- Road furniture maintenance (signs, guardrails, delineators, road markings)
- Shoulder maintenance (grading and compaction)
- Edge repair and reinstatement

**Periodic Maintenance Treatments (RDM 5.2 Chapter 11):**
- Surface dressing (single or double seal) -- for surface distress on sound pavement (RDM 5.2 Section 11.2.1)
- Emulsion slurry seal -- thin application for surface restoration (Section 11.2.2)
- Fog spray or surface enrichment -- for aged/brittle surfaces (Section 11.2.3)
- Crack sealing for structural cracks (Section 11.3)
- Thin overlay (30-50mm AC) -- for moderate rutting and cracking
- Gravel resheeting -- standard periodic treatment for unpaved roads (Ch 14)
- Inlays -- mill and replace to same level (Section 11.5)

**Rehabilitation (RDM 5.2 Chapter 9):**
- Structural overlays designed per RDM 5.2 Chapter 9 (catalogue method, M-E method, AASHTO SN method, deflection reduction method)
- Foamed bitumen recycling (Section 11.4.1)
- Reprocessing (Section 11.7)
- Full reconstruction (Section 11.11)
- Designed per Skill #25 Kenya module (cross-reference)

**Performance-Based Maintenance Contracts (OPRC/PBMC):**
KeNHA uses OPRC for major corridor maintenance. The contractor is responsible for maintaining a road section to specified performance standards (IRI, PCI thresholds) for a contract period (typically 5-7 years). Payment is based on performance compliance, not quantities. This model has been applied on several KeNHA corridors.

### M.2.3 Unpaved Road Maintenance (RDM 5.2 Chapter 14)

**Failure Criteria for Unpaved Roads (Table 14.2):**

| Parameter | Acceptable | Failure | Unit |
|-----------|-----------|---------|------|
| Gravel loss | < 25mm/year | > 25mm/year | mm |
| Corrugation depth | < 25 | > 50 | mm |
| Rut depth | < 25 | > 75 | mm |
| Pothole depth | < 50 | > 150 | mm |
| IRI | < 8 | > 14 | m/km |

**Gravel Resheeting:**
- Performance-based specifications from RDM 5.2 Section 14.4.2
- Typical resheeting thickness: 150mm compacted (Kenya standard practice)
- Maintenance grading cycle: every 2-4 months depending on traffic and rainfall
- Regravelling cycle: every 5-8 years depending on traffic and material quality

### M.2.4 Climate Resilience in Maintenance (RDM 5.2 Chapter 12)

RDM 5.2 Chapter 12 addresses climate resilience for maintenance and rehabilitation, including:
- Climate risk mapping for Kenya (flood risk areas, temperature risk areas -- Figures 12.2, 12.3)
- Catalogue of climate resilience solutions (Tables 12.1-12.3): subgrade materials, base/subbase materials, surfacing/wearing course adaptations
- Armoured pavement bases and amalgamated surfacing for climate-exposed roads
- Do-Nou technology for soft ground improvement on low-volume roads (Section 14.5)

## M.3 Country-Specific Reasoning

### M.3.1 Decision Logic (Country Branch)

**Kenya-specific branching:**
```
Determine road authority and surface type:
  |-- KeNHA trunk road (paved):
  |     Use RDM 5.2 Table 7.1 surface condition criteria
  |     FWD deflection data for structural assessment
  |     OPRC contract may apply -- check performance standards
  |     Budget from RMLF (KeNHA allocation)
  |
  |-- KeRRA rural road (paved):
  |     Use simplified condition criteria
  |     Visual assessment likely (limited FWD availability)
  |     Budget from RMLF (KeRRA allocation)
  |
  |-- KeRRA rural road (unpaved):
  |     Use RDM 5.2 Table 14.2 failure criteria
  |     Gravel resheeting when wearing course depleted
  |     Maintenance grading programme
  |     Budget from RMLF (KeRRA allocation)
  |
  |-- KURA urban road:
  |     Urban considerations per RDM 5.2 Ch 13
  |     Utility coordination required
  |     Higher unit costs in urban areas
  |
  +-- County road:
        Variable capacity -- recommend visual assessment triggers
        May require central government or donor support for rehabilitation
```

### M.3.2 Professional Judgment (Country-Specific)

**RMLF allocations are typically below maintenance needs.** The fuel levy generates significant revenue but the road network is vast. Prioritization (Skill #59) is essential to allocate limited funds effectively.

**Material availability varies regionally.** Hot mix asphalt is available near Nairobi, Mombasa, Kisumu, and other major towns. Remote areas may rely on surface dressing or cold mix. Gravel quality varies significantly -- some regions have excellent laterite, others have marginal materials requiring stabilisation.

**Overloading on the Northern Corridor accelerates structural distress.** When selecting rehabilitation treatments for trunk roads with known overloading, the maintenance plan should note that standard overlay thickness may be inadequate without vehicle load control measures.

**Drainage repair is critical before surface treatment.** RDM 5.2 Section 8.2 explicitly requires drainage restoration before overlay works. Failure to address drainage leads to premature failure of rehabilitation investment.

### M.3.3 Common Errors (Country-Specific)

1. **Planning maintenance without considering RMLF budget constraints.** A technically correct maintenance plan that exceeds the available RMLF allocation is not actionable. Always relate the plan to available budget and prioritize.
2. **Applying paved road treatments to unpaved roads.** Reseal or overlay is not appropriate for unpaved roads. Unpaved roads require gravel resheeting, reshaping, and drainage improvement per RDM 5.2 Chapter 14.
3. **Not including drainage in the maintenance plan.** RDM 5.2 Section 8.2 requires drainage assessment and repair before overlay. Every maintenance plan should include drainage condition assessment and repair activities.
4. **Ignoring climate resilience requirements.** RDM 5.2 Chapter 12 requires climate risk consideration for maintenance planning. Flood-prone and temperature-exposed sections require adapted treatment approaches.

### M.5.2 Standards Compliance Checks

- **Check:** Treatment type matches road surface type
- **Standard:** RDM 5.2
- **Pass condition:** Paved treatments for paved roads, unpaved treatments per Ch 14 for unpaved roads
- **Fail action:** SENIOR REVIEW -- Treatment type does not match road surface type. Verify.

- **Check:** Drainage assessed and included in maintenance plan
- **Standard:** RDM 5.2 Section 8.2
- **Pass condition:** Drainage condition assessed and drainage maintenance included before overlay
- **Fail action:** NOTE -- RDM 5.2 Section 8.2 requires drainage restoration before overlay works. Drainage not addressed in maintenance plan.

### M.5.3 Departures / Relaxations Process

RDM 5.2 states users must follow standards and seek Ministry approval for departures. Significant changes to maintenance treatment selection should be documented and approved by the responsible road authority's Chief Engineer.

## M.7 Worked Examples -- Kenya

### Example 1: KeNHA Class A Road -- Annual Maintenance Plan

**Project:** Maintenance planning for 15km section of KeNHA Class A road (Northern Corridor).

**Given:**
- Condition from Skill #56 Kenya module: Fair (IRI = 5.5 m/km, PCI = 55%)
- Distress summary: crocodile cracking at severity 3, extent 2; rutting 18mm average; potholes at severity 2, extent 1
- Road surface: Paved (AC), 7m carriageway, 2 lanes
- Traffic: 5,000 vpd (heavy commercial traffic)

**Solution:**

Step 1: Check intervention criteria (RDM 5.2 Table 7.1):
- IRI 5.5 falls in periodic range (4-6 m/km) --> Periodic TRIGGERED
- PCI 55% falls in periodic range (25-60%) --> Periodic TRIGGERED
- Rutting 18mm falls in periodic range (10-20mm) --> Periodic TRIGGERED
- Cracking severity 3 with extent 2 = DI 6 (moderate)

Step 2: Intervention level: **Periodic maintenance** (multiple periodic triggers exceeded but not yet rehabilitation)

Step 3: Treatment assessment:
- Structural investigation recommended (FWD deflection testing per RDM 5.2 Section 6.3)
- Rutting approaching rehabilitation threshold
- Drainage condition must be assessed before surface treatment (RDM 5.2 Section 8.2)

Step 4: Recommended treatments:
- Pre-treatment: pothole patching of worst failures (immediate, routine)
- Drainage: check and repair side drains, clean culverts before overlay
- Periodic treatment: thin overlay (40-50mm AC) on 15km x 7m = 105,000 m2
- Alternative: if deflections indicate structural inadequacy, refer to Skill #25 for structural overlay design

**Result:** Periodic maintenance triggered. Preliminary scope: 105,000 m2 AC overlay (thickness subject to deflection testing and Skill #25 design). Drainage repair must precede surface treatment per RDM 5.2. KeNHA responsible authority. Refer to Skill #50 for cost estimate with quantities. Note heavy commercial traffic -- overloading assessment recommended.
