### M.1.2 Institutional Context

Terrain classification in Kenya follows RDM definitions for all road authorities: KeNHA (national highways, Class A/B), KeRRA (rural roads, Class C/D/E and specialist classes), and KURA (urban roads, Class UA/UC/UL), plus 47 County Governments. The same four-class terrain system applies regardless of the responsible authority or funding source. Terrain classification is established before road design begins and is independent of the alignment finally selected for the road (RDM 1.3, Section 3.3.6).

## M.2 Country-Specific Knowledge

### M.2.1 Kenya Terrain Thresholds

The Kenya RDM defines terrain based on **transverse ground slopes perpendicular to the ground contours**. Kenya uses four terrain classes, with thresholds that differ from Uganda MoWT values.

| Terrain Class | Transverse Ground Slope | Description |
|---------------|------------------------|-------------|
| Flat | Generally below 3% | Sight distances generally long. Both horizontal and vertical restrictions are generally long or could be made to be so without construction difficulty or expense. |
| Rolling | Generally between 4% and 25% | Natural slopes consistently rise and fall above and below the road or street grade. Occasional steep slopes offer some restrictions to normal horizontal and vertical roadway alignment. Cross slopes perpendicular to contours generally 3-25%. |
| Mountainous | Generally between 26% and 50% | Route location and certain design features may be almost entirely governed by the terrain. Longitudinal and transverse changes in elevation are abrupt. Benching and side hill excavation are frequently required. Cross slopes perpendicular to contours generally 25-50%. |
| Escarpment | Generally greater than 50% | Escarpments are geological features that require special geometric standards because of the engineering problems involved. Cross slopes perpendicular to ground contours generally greater than 50%. |

**Source:** Kenya RDM 1.3, Table 3.1 and Sections 3.3.6.2-3.3.6.5 (Verified from PDF)

**Alternative terrain classification (from RDM 1.3 Glossary and RDM 1.1):**

| Terrain Class | Contours Crossed per km (5 m contours) |
|---------------|---------------------------------------|
| Flat | 0-10 |
| Rolling | 11-25 |
| Mountainous | > 25 |

**Source:** Kenya RDM 1.3, Glossary of Terms, "Terrain Classification" entry (Verified from PDF)

**Comparison with Uganda MoWT terrain thresholds:**

| Terrain Class | Kenya RDM 1.3 (2025) | Uganda MoWT (2010) |
|---------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Flat | Below 3% | Around 5% |
| Rolling | 4-25% (or 3-25% per body text) | > 5% to 20% |
| Mountainous | 26-50% (or 25-50% per body text) | > 20% to 70% |
| Escarpment | > 50% | > 70% |

The Kenya and Uganda thresholds are **not interchangeable**. Kenya's flat threshold is lower (3% vs 5%), rolling range is wider (up to 25% vs up to 20%), and escarpment threshold is lower (50% vs 70%). Always use the Kenya-specific thresholds when the project is in Kenya.

**Note on gradient limits in mountainous terrain:** Kenya RDM 1.3 Section 3.3.6 states it is difficult to provide adequate compaction on gradients greater than 10%. Where higher gradients cannot be avoided, they should be restricted in length. Gradients greater than 10% should not be longer than 250 m. Horizontal curve radii of as little as 13 m may also sometimes be unavoidable, even though a minimum of 15 m is specified.

### M.2.2 Design Speed Implications by Terrain

Terrain class directly determines design speed via the Kenya RDM design speed tables. For reference (full tables in Skill #13 Kenya module):

| Functional Class | Flat | Rolling | Mountainous | Escarpment |
|-----------------|------|---------|-------------|------------|
| A | 110-120 km/h | 90-100 km/h | 70-80 km/h | - |
| B | 90-110 km/h | 85-90 km/h | 60-70 km/h | 50 km/h |
| C | 80-100 km/h | 65-90 km/h | 50-70 km/h | 50 km/h |
| D | 70-90 km/h | 65-85 km/h | 50-60 km/h | 40-50 km/h |
| E | 50-70 km/h | 40-65 km/h | 30-50 km/h | 30 km/h |

**Source:** Kenya RDM 1.3, Table 3.4 (see Skill #13 Kenya module for full parameter tables)

**Terrain classification affects design standard selection:**

- For KeNHA roads (Class A/B), terrain determines whether expressway or standard arterial design speeds apply.
- For KeRRA roads (Class D/E), terrain determines whether mountainous design standards with reduced speeds and tighter curves are required.
- For KURA urban roads, terrain classification is less relevant as urban design speeds are governed by the urban environment rather than terrain (Table 3.5).
