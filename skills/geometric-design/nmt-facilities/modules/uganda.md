### M.1.2 Institutional Context

Same as other Uganda modules. MoWT Road Design Manual is the national standard. Section 11 covers NMT facilities and Section 10 covers speed management. Both are relatively brief (combined 12 pages) compared to the detailed treatment of geometric design for motorised traffic. This means supplementary guidance from international practice is needed in several areas, and all supplementary content is flagged clearly as non-MoWT.

### M.1.3 Standard Philosophy

MoWT provides basic dimensional requirements for footways, cycleways, crossings, and speed management measures. The standard is prescriptive on minimum dimensions but silent on several NMT topics common in East Africa (boda-boda facilities, handcart accommodation, detailed accessibility). The manual's NMT guidance should be read as minimum requirements that should be enhanced based on local pedestrian and cyclist volumes.

## M.2 Country-Specific Knowledge

### M.2.1 Design Tables

**Table UN.1: Footway Provision Criteria (Table 11-1, p241, Verified)**

| Location | Average Daily Vehicle Traffic | Pedestrian flow/day (60-80 km/h) | Pedestrian flow/day (80-100 km/h) |
|----------|------------------------------|----------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| One side only | 400-1,400 | 300 | 200 |
| One side only | >1,400 | 200 | 120 |
| Both sides | 700-1,400 | 1,000 | 600 |
| Both sides | >1,400 | 600 | 400 |

**Interpretation:** At higher road speeds (80-100 km/h), lower pedestrian thresholds trigger footway provision because the consequences of pedestrian-vehicle conflict are more severe.

**Table UN.2: Footway Widths (Section 11.1.1, p241, Verified)**

| Category | Width (m) | Description |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Absolute minimum | 1.0 | Two persons cannot pass -- emergency use only |
| Desirable minimum | 1.8 | Two persons can pass closely |
| Light volume | 2.25 | Comfortable passing, suitable for moderate flows |
| Heavy volume | 3.5+ | Space for three abreast, high-flow trading centres |

**Footway Design (Sections 7.15, 11.1.1, Verified):**
- Rural: separated from carriageway by grass strip >= 2.0 m, gradient < 4%
- Urban: raised kerbed footways, kerb height 100-150 mm (200 mm to deter parking)
- On bridges: minimum 1.0 m wide footway, pedestrian parapet, segregated by safety barrier on Class A and B roads

**Table UN.3: Subway Dimensions (Table 11-2, p244, Verified)**

| Type | Width (m) | Height (m) |
|------|-----------|------------|
| Narrow (short subway) | 2.3 | 2.3 |
| Standard | 3.3 | 2.6 |
| Wide (long subway) | 5.0 | 2.6 |

**Footbridge Parameters (Section 11.1.3, p243, Verified):**
- Clearance above carriageway: **5.0 m** minimum
- Ramp gradient: maximum **5%** (maximum **3%** if cyclists use the ramp)
- Stair dimensions: tread **300 mm**, riser **130 mm**

**Crossing Design (Section 11.1.2, p242, Verified):**
- Refuge islands: **1.5-2.0 m** wide (absolute minimum **1.2 m**)
- Minimum carriageway width for one lane of traffic past refuge: **3.5-4.0 m** (4.5 m if cyclists present)
- Humped pedestrian crossings recommended (plateau hump with crossing on the flat top)
- Build-outs to reduce crossing distance

**Table UN.4: Cycleway Widths (Table 11-3, p246, Verified)**

| Type | Minimum (m) | Standard (m) | Heavy usage (m) |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Cycleway (separate) | 2.0 | 2.5 | 3.5 |
| Combined cycleway and footway | 2.0 | 3.0 | 4.5 |
| Cycle lane (one way) | 1.5 | 2.0 | 2.5 |

**Cyclist Dimensions (Figure 11-5, p246, Verified):**
- Width: 0.75 m
- Length: 2.00 m
- Height: 1.90 m

**Table UN.5: Cyclist Clearances (Table 11-4, p246, Verified)**

| Clearance Type | Distance (m) |
|---------------|-------------|
| Minimum overhead clearance | 0.50 |
| To wall/fence/barrier | 0.50 |
| To unfenced drop-off | 1.0 |
| To traffic lane edge at 50 km/h | 0.50 |
| To traffic lane edge at 80 km/h | 1.00 |
| To traffic lane edge at 100 km/h | 1.50 |

**Bus Lay-by (Section 11.2, pp. 244-245, Verified):**
- Entry taper: **15 m**
- Exit taper: **20 m**
- Entry radius: **18 m**
- Exit radius: **12 m**
- Standing length: minimum **15 m** per bus

**Speed Management (Section 10, pp. 235-240, Verified):**

Town speed limit: **50 km/h** or less.

**Gate Design (Section 10.1):**
- Entry radius R1 < **100 m** for 50 km/h speed control
- Entry radius R1 < **50 m** for 30 km/h speed control

**Speed Humps (Section 10.2):**
- Circular hump: 100 mm height, 3-12 m length
- Plateau hump: 100 mm height, 4-6 m flat top

**Speed Hump Intervals (Section 10.3):**
- 50 m spacing for 30 km/h target areas
- 125 m spacing for 50 km/h target areas

### M.2.3 East African NMT Context

This section covers NMT modes common in East Africa that are not addressed by MoWT. **All content in this section is supplementary and must be clearly identified as such.**

---

**[Supplementary -- not from MoWT]**

### Boda-bodas (Motorcycle Taxis)

Boda-bodas are the dominant urban and peri-urban transport mode in Uganda. They carry passengers and goods on motorcycles, operating from informal staging areas near junctions, markets, and trading centres. MoWT does not provide any guidance on boda-boda facilities -- no staging area design, parking dimensions, or dedicated lane guidance exists in the standard.

**Practical design considerations (practice-based guidance, NOT from MoWT standard):**
- **Staging areas:** Boda-boda staging areas need approximately 20-30 m2 for a group of 5-10 motorcycles. Locate near junctions but clear of visibility splays (Skill #19). A paved surface is recommended to prevent dust and mud.
- **Separation from pedestrians:** Where pedestrian and boda-boda movements overlap (common at trading centres), provide physical separation (kerbs, bollards) to protect pedestrians.
- **Junction design:** At junctions with heavy boda-boda traffic, consider wider left-turn radii and queuing space at stop lines (boda-bodas queue ahead of other traffic).
- **Speed environment:** Boda-bodas operate at 30-60 km/h in urban areas. Speed management measures (humps, gates) affect boda-bodas as well as other vehicles.

**[Supplementary -- not from MoWT]**

### Handcarts and Wheelbarrows

Handcarts and wheelbarrows are common transport modes in Ugandan trading centres, used for moving goods from markets to shops and loading areas. MoWT does not address these modes.

**Practical design considerations (practice-based guidance, NOT from MoWT standard):**
- **Minimum path width:** 2.0 m for a handcart to pass a pedestrian
- **Avoid steps and high kerbs:** Handcarts cannot negotiate steps. Dropped kerbs or flush surfaces are essential at crossing points.
- **Maximum gradient:** 5% for loaded handcarts
- **Surface quality:** Paved or well-maintained gravel. Loose gravel and potholes are problematic for wheeled handcarts.
- **Combined use with footways:** Where separate facilities are not feasible, footways of 3.0 m+ can accommodate mixed pedestrian/handcart movement.

**[Supplementary -- not from MoWT]**

### High Pedestrian Volumes

Uganda's roads carry far more pedestrians than Western design standards assume. In rural trading centres, pedestrian flows of 500-2000 per day on trunk roads are common. The MoWT "heavy volume" footway width of 3.5 m+ should be the **starting point** in trading centres, not the exception. Design for actual observed pedestrian volumes, not for minimum standard requirements.

**Key consideration:** Pedestrian volumes peak at market days (often twice weekly) and school hours. Design should accommodate peak flows, not average daily flows only.

## M.3 Country-Specific Reasoning

### M.3.1 Decision Logic (Uganda Branch)

```
Step 2 (Uganda): Footway provision
  |-- Look up Table UN.1 using AADT and pedestrian flow/day
  |-- If AADT > 1400 and ped flow > 400/day (80-100 km/h) --> Both sides
  |-- If AADT > 1400 and ped flow > 120/day (80-100 km/h) --> One side
  |-- If road passes through trading centre --> Both sides regardless (professional judgment)
  +-- If near school --> At least one side, with crossing provision

Step 3 (Uganda): Footway width
  |-- If trading centre / heavy volume --> 3.5 m+ (Table UN.2)
  |-- If moderate pedestrian flow --> 2.25 m
  |-- If light pedestrian flow --> 1.8 m (desirable minimum)
  +-- Never use 1.0 m (absolute minimum) for new designs

Step 6 (Uganda): Speed management
  |-- If road passes through trading centre --> Speed management required
  |-- Gate design at town entry (R1 per target speed)
  |-- Speed humps at intervals per target speed
  +-- Humped pedestrian crossings at key crossing points
```

### M.3.2 Professional Judgment (Uganda-Specific)

**Trading centres are the critical context.** Most pedestrian casualties on Uganda's trunk roads occur in and near trading centres where pedestrian volumes are high and traffic speeds are still excessive. The combination of footway provision, crossing facilities, and speed management is the most important NMT design decision.

**Do not design rural road NMT as an afterthought.** Even on rural roads, trading centres, schools, and health facilities generate significant pedestrian traffic. The cross-section (Skill #17) must include footway width from the outset -- it is extremely difficult to retrofit footways within an existing road reserve.

**Boda-boda staging areas need planning.** If not planned, boda-boda riders will create informal staging areas that obstruct visibility, block footways, and create conflict with pedestrians. Designating staging areas in the design is far better than dealing with informal occupation later.

### M.3.3 Common Errors (Uganda-Specific)

1. **Designing footways as afterthought.** Footways must be integral to the cross-section from the start, consistent with Skill #17 outputs.
2. **Ignoring high pedestrian volumes in rural trading centres.** A rural road with AADT 1500 may have 1000 pedestrians/day at a trading centre. The footway provision criteria (Table UN.1) are triggered.
3. **Using absolute minimum footway width (1.0 m) for new construction.** The 1.0 m width is for constrained retrofit situations only. New designs should use at least 1.8 m (desirable minimum) and preferably 2.25 m+.
4. **Not providing speed management at trading centres.** MoWT Section 10 requires speed reduction to 50 km/h through trading centres. Gates and humps are the geometric tools for achieving this.
5. **Failing to coordinate crossing locations with desire lines.** Crossings placed at geometrically convenient locations but away from where people actually walk will be ignored.

### M.5.2 Standards Compliance Checks

- **Check:** Footway provision meets Table UN.1 criteria
- **Standard:** MoWT Vol 1, Table 11-1
- **Pass condition:** Footway provided where AADT and pedestrian flow exceed thresholds
- **Fail action:** SENIOR REVIEW -- Footway required per MoWT Table 11-1 but not provided.

- **Check:** Speed management provided through trading centres
- **Standard:** MoWT Vol 1, Section 10
- **Pass condition:** Gates and/or humps specified for trading centre sections
- **Fail action:** Flag -- Speed management required through trading centres per MoWT Section 10.

- **Check:** Crossing facilities provided where pedestrian flows are high
- **Standard:** MoWT Vol 1, Section 11.1.2
- **Pass condition:** Formal crossing specified where pedestrian flows warrant
- **Fail action:** SENIOR REVIEW -- High pedestrian flow [X]/day with no formal crossing provision.

### M.5.3 Departures / Relaxations Process

MoWT does not have a formal departures process. Where the standard cannot be met (e.g., insufficient road reserve for standard footway width), the engineer documents the justification in the design report and consults with the Engineer-In-Chief, MoWT.

## M.7 Worked Example -- Uganda

### Example: Class II Paved Road Through a Trading Centre

**Given:**
- Road: Class II Paved, design speed 50 km/h (through trading centre section)
- Main road design speed outside trading centre: 80 km/h
- AADT: 2000
- Pedestrian flow: 800 per day at the trading centre
- Trading centre length: approximately 600 m
- No existing footway or crossing provision

**Step 1 -- Footway provision:**
From Table UN.1:
- AADT = 2000 (>1,400 row)
- Pedestrian flow = 800/day at 60-80 km/h threshold column
- For "both sides": threshold is 600 ped/day when AADT >1,400 at 60-80 km/h
- 800 > 600 --> **Footways on both sides**

**Step 2 -- Footway width:**
From Table UN.2:
- Pedestrian flow 800/day is moderate to heavy for a trading centre
- Select **2.25 m** (light/moderate volume -- adequate for 800/day)
- Consider upgrading to 3.5 m if market days produce peak flows exceeding 1500/day

**Step 3 -- Speed management:**
Road enters trading centre at 80 km/h design speed. Must reduce to 50 km/h.

**Gate design:**
- Entry radius R1 < 100 m for 50 km/h target speed
- Provide entry gates at both ends of the trading centre

**Speed humps:**
- 50 km/h target --> spacing 125 m (MoWT Section 10.3)
- Over 600 m trading centre length: approximately 4-5 humps
- Use **plateau humps** (100 mm height, 4-6 m flat top) at crossing locations

**Step 4 -- Crossing provision:**
With 800 pedestrians/day and AADT 2000 at 50 km/h:
- Provide **humped pedestrian crossings** at key desire lines (market area, school access)
- Recommended: at least 2 formal crossings over 600 m
- Each crossing should be a plateau hump with marked crossing on the flat top

**Step 5 -- Refuge islands:**
Carriageway width for Class II Paved in town = 12.0 m (from Skill #17, Figure 7-16).
- 12.0 m is wide for an unassisted crossing
- Provide **refuge islands** (1.5 m wide) at crossing locations
- Carriageway width per lane past refuge: (12.0 - 1.5) / 2 = 5.25 m --> adequate (>3.5 m minimum)

**Result:**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Footway | Both sides, 2.25 m wide (minimum) |
| Kerb height | 150 mm |
| Speed management | Entry gates (R1 < 100 m) at both ends |
| Speed humps | Plateau, 100 mm, at approximately 125 m intervals (4-5 humps) |
| Crossings | 2 humped pedestrian crossings at desire lines |
| Refuge islands | 1.5 m wide at each crossing |
| Trading centre speed | 50 km/h |
