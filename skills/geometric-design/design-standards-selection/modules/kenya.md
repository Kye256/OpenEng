Note: The Kenya RDM was developed by TRL Limited (UK) for the Ministry of Roads and Transport (MoRT), with financial support from the African Development Bank. It replaces the first-generation manuals developed in the first and second decades after independence. The RDM is a comprehensive series covering all stages of the road project cycle from planning through maintenance (7 volumes, 30+ parts). RDM 1.3 is the primary source for geometric design standards, road classification, and design speed selection.

### M.1.2 Institutional Context

Kenya has three road authorities under the Ministry of Roads and Transport (MoRT), plus 47 County Governments. All use the Kenya Road Design Manual:

- **KeNHA (Kenya National Highways Authority):** Manages national trunk roads (Class A and B -- international and national highways). Uses the RDM as the mandatory design standard. KeNHA roads include expressways (sub-category of Class A with full access control).
- **KeRRA (Kenya Rural Roads Authority):** Manages rural roads (Class C, D, E, and specialist rural classes F, G, K, L, P, R, S, T, U, W). Uses the RDM as the mandatory design standard.
- **KURA (Kenya Urban Roads Authority):** Manages urban roads (Class UA, UC, UL). Uses the RDM as the mandatory design standard.
- **47 County Governments:** Manage county roads under devolved functions per the Constitution of Kenya 2010. Use the RDM, though engineering capacity varies significantly between counties. Class D and E roads are located within counties and numbered using county codes.

**Kenya Roads Act 2007** (and Urban Areas and Cities Act 2011) established the three road authorities. Road classification is managed by the Chief Engineer, Roads under MoRT.

**Funding source:** Projects funded by development partners (World Bank, AfDB, JICA) follow the same RDM but may specify additional requirements in Terms of Reference. The AfDB funded the development of the RDM 2025 series.

### M.1.3 Standard Philosophy

**Kenya RDM (2025):** This document has the status of a Manual. Users shall apply the contents there-in to fully satisfy the requirements set out (RDM 1.3, Document Management). The RDM uses mandatory "shall" language throughout -- this is more prescriptive than the Uganda MoWT Road Design Manual (2010) which uses advisory "should" language.

**Departures from standards:** Where the designer departs from a standard, written approval must be obtained from the **Chief Engineer for Roads** (RDM 1.3, Section 1.7). The designer shall submit:
1. The number, name, and description of the road section
2. The design parameter for which a Departure from Standards is desired
3. A description of the standard, including normal value, and the value of the Departure
4. The reason for the Departure from Standards
5. Any mitigation to be applied in the interests of safety
6. Justification for the departure

This is a formal process -- significantly more prescriptive than Uganda MoWT's informal "consultation with the Engineer-In-Chief" approach. The approval may involve a consultative process in accordance with the procedures and guidelines manual or statutes governing public consultations.

**Note on expressways:** Expressways are a special type of roadway within the functional classification system. They are the highest level of arterial, characterised by full control of access, high design speeds, and a high level of driver comfort and safety (RDM 1.3, Section 2.2.2). Expressways are a sub-category within Class A, not a separate road class. The design standard DR1 applies to expressways (DHV > 8000, divided carriageway with full access control).

**Note on "Class S" terminology:** The PLAN anticipated a Class S for expressways. However, the actual Kenya RDM Table 2.1 uses "S" as a rural specialist class for Sugar Roads. Expressways are handled as a sub-type within Class A using design standard DR1. The urban classes are UA, UC, UL (not UA, UB, UC as anticipated). This module uses the actual RDM classification.

No Kenya-specific formulas for design standards selection beyond the universal traffic projection formula in the core skill. The design speed selection is table-driven from Tables 3.4 and 3.5.

### M.2.3 Classification System

Kenya uses a **combined functional classification and administrative categories** system:

**Interurban roads (Classes A, B, C):** Major arterial roads linking internationally, nationally, and regionally important centres. These are managed by KeNHA and carry through-traffic across urban and rural areas. Class A includes expressways as a sub-category (design standard DR1 with full access control and divided carriageway).

**Rural roads (Classes D, E, and specialist classes):** Collector and local roads within counties. Classes D and E are major and minor collector roads respectively. Classes F through W are specialist local rural roads serving specific land uses (forests, coffee, tea, sugar, wheat, game parks, settlements, government facilities). These are managed by KeRRA and County Governments.

**Urban roads (Classes UA, UC, UL):** Urban Arterial, Urban Collector, and Local Urban Streets. These are managed by KURA and serve traffic within municipalities and cities. Design standards DU1-DU6 apply.

**The mapping between classification and design standard is not one-to-one.** For example, a Class B road could use design standard DR2 or DR3 depending on traffic volume. A Class C road could use DR3 or DR4. The design standard is selected based on both functional class AND design traffic (AADT or DHV).

**Route numbering:** Kenya uses an alphanumeric system where the letter prefix indicates the road class (A, B, C, D, E, etc.) followed by a route number. Expressways within Class A and B have an additional "E" suffix (e.g., A104-E2 for an expressway on the A104 corridor). County codes per the First Schedule of the Constitution of Kenya 2010 are embedded in D, E, and R class road numbers.

## M.3 Country-Specific Reasoning

### M.3.1 Decision Logic (Kenya Branch)

```
Step 2 (Kenya): Determine which standard
  +-- Kenya RDM is the mandatory standard for ALL classified roads in Kenya
  +-- If road is under KeNHA (Class A, B national highways)
  |     -> Primary: Kenya RDM 1.3 (2025)
  |     -> Design standards DR1-DR3 (high-volume arterials)
  +-- If road is under KeRRA (Class C, D, E rural roads)
  |     -> Primary: Kenya RDM 1.3 (2025)
  |     -> Design standards DR3-DR7
  +-- If road is under KURA (Class UA, UC, UL urban roads)
  |     -> Primary: Kenya RDM 1.3 (2025), Chapters 16-17
  |     -> Design standards DU1-DU6
  +-- If road is under County Government
  |     -> Primary: Kenya RDM 1.3 (2025)
  |     -> Check county engineering capacity -- may need KeRRA support
  +-- If funded by development partner
  |     -> Check: ToR may specify additional requirements beyond RDM
  \-- If specialist rural road (F, G, K, L, P, R, S, T, U, W)
        -> RDM Design Standard DR7; also consider specific land use requirements

Step 6a (Kenya -- RDM): Determine Functional Class
  +-- From road's role in the network (see Table K.1):
  |     +-- Links international centres / Trans-Africa Highway -> Class A
  |     +-- Links nationally important centres -> Class B
  |     +-- Links regionally important centres across counties -> Class C
  |     +-- Major collector within a county -> Class D
  |     +-- Minor collector within a sub-county -> Class E
  |     +-- Specialist land-use access road -> Class F/G/K/L/P/R/S/T/U/W
  |     +-- Urban arterial -> Class UA
  |     +-- Urban collector -> Class UC
  |     +-- Local urban street -> Class UL
  \-- If unclear -> Ask user. Default to Class D for county collector roads.

Step 6b (Kenya -- RDM): Determine Design Standard
  +-- Rural roads: from functional class + traffic volume:
  |     +-- Class A, DHV > 8000 -> DR1 (expressway/divided carriageway)
  |     +-- Class A/B, AADT > 4000 or DHV 500-8000 -> DR2
  |     +-- Class B/C, AADT 2000-4000 or DHV 250-500 -> DR3
  |     +-- Class B/C, AADT 500-2000 -> DR4
  |     +-- Class D, AADT 150-500 -> DR5
  |     +-- Class E, AADT 50-150 -> DR6
  |     +-- Class F-W, AADT < 50 -> DR7
  +-- Urban roads: from functional class + traffic volume per lane:
  |     +-- Class UA, > 12,000 per lane -> DU1
  |     +-- Class UA, 6,000-12,000 per lane -> DU2
  |     +-- Class UC, 4,000-6,000 per lane -> DU3
  |     +-- Class UC, 2,000-4,000 per lane -> DU4
  |     +-- Class UL, 500-2,000 per lane -> DU5
  |     +-- Class UL, < 500 per lane -> DU6
  \-- Note: Traffic-based selection must be cross-checked against functional class

Step 7 (Kenya): Determine design speed
  +-- Rural roads: from functional class + terrain (Table K.4)
  |     -> Select within the max-min range for the terrain and class
  +-- Urban roads: from functional class (Table K.5)
  |     -> Select maximum or reduced speed based on context
  \-- If expressway (DR1): design speed per Chapter 14 (interurban design standards)

Step 8 (Kenya): Design vehicle
  +-- High volume roads (Class A, B) -> DV5 (truck + semi-trailer) or DV6
  +-- Intermediate roads (Class C, D) -> DV4 (single unit truck) or DV5
  +-- Low volume roads (Class E, specialist) -> DV4 or DV1 (but accommodate occasional DV4)
  +-- Urban arterial (UA) -> DV5 (accommodate articulated vehicles)
  +-- Urban collector/local (UC, UL) -> DV2 (bus) or DV4 (truck)
  \-- Note: For high volume roads in Kenya, the design vehicle should be a truck and trailer except for very severe escarpment terrain (RDM 1.3, Section 3.5)

Step 9 (Kenya): Design period
  +-- All new paved roads -> 20 years (Section 3.4: "a design life of 20 years shall be adopted")
  +-- Gravel roads -> 10 years
  +-- Rehabilitation/reconstruction -> 20 years
```

### M.3.2 Professional Judgment (Kenya-Specific)

**The Kenya RDM is mandatory, but practical judgment is still needed within the standards.** Design speed tables provide a range (maximum and minimum) for each terrain class, allowing the designer to select an appropriate speed within that range based on traffic volume, alignment constraints, and cost considerations.

**Expressway design is new in Kenya.** The Nairobi Expressway (opened 2022) is Kenya's first expressway. The RDM 2025 includes expressway standards (DR1, Chapter 14) but Kenya has limited field experience with expressway design and operation. Designers should exercise particular care with expressway access control, interchange design, and tolling infrastructure.

**County engineering capacity varies widely.** Some of the 47 counties have experienced road engineers; others do not. For projects in counties with limited capacity, the designer should provide more detailed specifications and consider simpler design standards that can be supervised effectively.

**Motorcycle (boda-boda) traffic is significant.** Kenya's motorcycle fleet has grown exponentially. The RDM specifically addresses motorcycle design considerations (Section 3.6) including motorcycle-friendly safety barriers, adequate clear zones, and surface consistency. This is particularly relevant for Class D and E roads where motorcycle traffic may exceed motorised vehicle traffic.

**Climate change considerations.** The RDM 2025 explicitly incorporates climate change considerations throughout the design process (Foreword). This is relevant to design life selection and drainage sizing but less directly to classification.

### M.3.3 Common Errors (Kenya-Specific)

1. **Confusing Kenya Class A with Uganda Functional Class A.** In Kenya, Class A means "International Highways" (Trans-Africa, Eastern Africa Highway routes). In Uganda, Functional Class A also means "International Trunk" but maps to different design classes (Ia, Ib). The classification systems are independent -- never cross-reference between countries.

2. **Treating expressways as a separate road class.** Kenya expressways are a sub-category within Class A, using design standard DR1. There is no separate "expressway class" in Table 2.1. The expressway designation comes from the design standard (DR1 with full access control and divided carriageway), not from a separate functional classification letter.

3. **Applying Uganda MoWT design speeds to Kenya road classes.** Kenya Class C on flat terrain has a design speed range of 80-100 km/h. Uganda Design Class II (which might serve similar functional roads) has 90 km/h on flat terrain. These are different standards with different values -- always use the Kenya table.

4. **Ignoring the specialist rural classes.** Kenya has 10+ specialist rural classes (F, G, K, L, P, R, S, T, U, W) that all map to design standard DR7 but have distinct administrative and land-use contexts. Do not classify a coffee road as Class E simply because it is low-volume -- the classification carries administrative and funding implications.

5. **Using the wrong traffic metric for urban roads.** Rural design standards use AADT or DHV (Design Hour Volume). Urban design standards (DU1-DU6) use ADT per lane, which is a different metric. Confusing these leads to incorrect design standard selection.

### M.5.2 Standards Compliance Checks

- **Check:** Design speed falls within the range specified for the assigned functional class and terrain.
- **Standard:** RDM 1.3, Table 3.4 (rural) or Table 3.5 (urban)
- **Pass condition:** Speed is between minimum and maximum values for the class and terrain
- **Fail action:** Warn -- "Design speed [X] km/h does not fall within the range for Class [Y] on [terrain] terrain. Expected range [Min]-[Max] km/h per RDM Table 3.4."

- **Check:** Design standard is appropriate for the functional class and traffic volume.
- **Standard:** RDM 1.3, Tables 2.7 and 2.8
- **Pass condition:** Design standard matches the functional class and traffic range
- **Fail action:** Warn -- "Design Standard [DRx] is not consistent with Functional Class [Y] and traffic volume [Z]. Review classification per RDM Tables 2.7/2.8."

- **Check:** Access control level is consistent with functional class.
- **Standard:** RDM 1.3, Tables 2.3 and 2.4
- **Pass condition:** Access control is at least at the "reduced" level for the functional class
- **Fail action:** Warn -- "Access control level for Class [Y] should be [level] per RDM Table 2.3."

### M.5.3 Departures / Relaxations Process

Kenya RDM has a **formal departures process** (Section 1.7). Where the designer departs from a standard, written approval shall be obtained from the Chief Engineer for Roads. The submission shall include: road identification, the parameter being departed from, the standard value and proposed departure value, reason, safety mitigation, and justification. This process may involve public consultation per the procedures and guidelines manual. This is a legally binding requirement -- departures without Chief Engineer approval are non-compliant.

## M.7 Worked Example -- Kenya

### Example 1: KeNHA Trunk Road (Class A)

**Source:** Hypothetical, based on typical KeNHA practice.

**Given:**
- Road: Nakuru-Eldoret Highway (A104)
- Country: Kenya
- Authority: KeNHA
- Function: National trunk road -- links regionally important centres along Trans-Africa Highway
- Terrain: Rolling (Rift Valley escarpment section with flat plateau sections)
- Current AADT: 4,500 vpd (2024 KeNHA count)
- Growth rate: 6% (KeNHA feasibility study)
- Urban/rural: Predominantly rural with town sections
- Funding: AfDB / Government of Kenya

**Solution:**

**Standard selection:** KeNHA road -> Kenya RDM 1.3 (mandatory). AfDB funding noted but ToR does not specify different standard.

**Terrain:** Rolling (predominant for the A104 corridor through the Rift Valley). Some flat sections on the plateau and mountainous sections at escarpment crossings.

**Design-year AADT:**
- Growth rate: 6% (from feasibility study)
- Design period: 20 years (RDM Section 3.4 -- paved road)
- Design-year AADT = 4,500 x (1.06)^20 = 4,500 x 3.207 = **14,432 vpd (2044)**

**Functional class:** Class A (International Highway -- Trans-Africa route, links Nakuru to Eldoret and onward to Uganda border)

**Design standard:** AADT > 4000 -> DR2 (RDM Table 2.7). However, the design-year AADT of 14,432 and DHV (assuming 10-12% of AADT) of approximately 1,400-1,700 vpd places this within the DR2 range. If the design-year DHV exceeds 8,000 (unlikely for this route), DR1 would apply.

**Design speed:** Class A, Rolling -> 90-100 km/h (Table K.4). Select 100 km/h for the plateau sections, 90 km/h for rolling sections.

**Design vehicle:** DV5 (Truck + semi-trailer) -- Class A trunk road with significant freight traffic.

**Result:**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Standard | Kenya RDM 1.3 (2025) |
| Functional Class | A (International Highway) |
| Design Standard | DR2 (single/dual carriageway, high standard) |
| Design Speed | 100 km/h (flat/plateau) / 90 km/h (rolling sections) |
| Design Vehicle | DV5 (Truck + semi-trailer) |
| Design Period | 20 years |
| Design Year AADT | 14,432 vpd |

**Escalation:** None -- all parameters within normal ranges for DR2. Note: if traffic growth exceeds 7%, the design-year AADT could exceed DR2/DR1 threshold. Consider staged construction to dual carriageway.
