### M.1.2 Institutional Context

RDM 2.2 Chapter 6 provides comprehensive erosion protection guidance for all Kenya road agencies (KeNHA, KeRRA, KURA, County Governments). The manual covers both river channel training (Section 6.2) for major waterway crossings and erosion control for road drainage features (Section 6.3). Bio-engineering is explicitly included as a sustainable erosion control technique (Section 6.3.6). For institutional context details, see Skill #13 Kenya module M.1.2.

### M.1.3 Standard Philosophy

RDM 2.2 takes a comprehensive approach to erosion protection, covering the full spectrum from simple stone pitching to complex river training structures. The manual emphasises locally available materials and includes bio-engineering as a sustainable, cost-effective option. The approach is consistent with East African practice while incorporating international best practices.

## M.2 Country-Specific Knowledge

### M.2.1 Design Tables

**River Channel Training and Revetment Measures (RDM 2.2 Section 6.2):**

Section 6.2 addresses river training structures for protecting road embankments and bridge approaches. Key topics include:

- **Probable problems** (Section 6.2.1): Bank erosion, bed degradation, channel migration, scour at structures
- **Criteria for choice** (Section 6.2.2): Selection based on flow velocity, bank height, soil type, available materials
- **Design of measures** (Section 6.2.3): Sizing of revetment elements, toe protection, filter requirements
- **Typical structures** (Section 6.2.4): Guide bunds, spurs/groynes, bank revetment, retards, dikes

**Revetment Structure Types (from RDM 2.2 Section 6.2.4):**

| Structure Type | Application | Typical Velocity Range |
|---------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Spur dikes/groynes | Redirect flow away from banks | 2-5 m/s |
| Guide bunds | Guide flow through bridge/culvert | 2-6 m/s |
| Bank revetment (stone) | Protect bank face | 2-4 m/s |
| Bank revetment (gabion) | Protect bank face, flexible | 3-5 m/s |
| Retards | Reduce velocity near banks | 1-3 m/s |
| Vegetation/bio-engineering | Long-term stabilisation | 0.5-2 m/s |

**Erosion Control Methods (RDM 2.2 Section 6.3):**

**Stone Pitching (Sections 6.3.1/6.3.2):**
- Hand-placed stone on prepared bed with mortar jointing
- Typical stone size: 150-300 mm
- Suitable for velocities up to 3.0 m/s
- Requires toe protection and filter layer
- Common for side drain lining, embankment slope protection, and culvert aprons

**Gabions (Section 6.3.3):**
- Wire mesh baskets filled with stone (100-250 mm)
- Standard sizes: 2.0 x 1.0 x 0.5 m or 2.0 x 1.0 x 1.0 m
- Suitable for velocities up to 5.0 m/s
- Flexible -- accommodate settlement and ground movement
- Mesh: galvanized or PVC-coated wire, hexagonal double-twist
- Suitable for bank protection, retaining walls, and energy dissipation

**Protection of Road Embankments (Section 6.3.4):**
- Slope protection using stone pitching, gabion mattresses, or vegetation
- Toe protection to prevent undermining
- Surface drainage to prevent slope saturation
- Critical for embankments adjacent to rivers and in flood-prone areas

**Lined Channels or Cascades (Section 6.3.5):**
- Concrete-lined channels for high-velocity flows
- Stepped cascades for steep terrain to dissipate energy
- Chute channels with energy dissipation at the base
- Typical for urban drainage outfalls and steep road sections

**Bio-engineering for Erosion Protection (Section 6.3.6):**
- Use of vegetation (grasses, shrubs, trees) alone or combined with structural measures
- Vetiver grass hedgerows: effective slope stabilisation, widely available in Kenya
- Live stakes: willow or similar species inserted into slopes
- Brush layering and fascines: for bank stabilisation
- Advantages: low cost, self-repairing, environmentally beneficial, uses local labour
- Limitations: requires establishment period (3-12 months before full effectiveness), not suitable for high-velocity zones (> 2 m/s without structural support)

**Permissible Velocities by Soil Type:**

| Soil Type | Permissible Velocity (m/s) |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| Fine sand, sandy loam | 0.3-0.6 |
| Silt loam | 0.6-0.8 |
| Firm clay, laterite | 0.8-1.5 |
| Coarse gravel | 1.2-2.0 |
| Cobbles, boulders | 2.5-4.0 |
| Rock (intact) | > 4.0 |

**Protection Type Selection by Velocity:**

| Velocity Range (m/s) | Recommended Protection | Kenya Materials |
|---------------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| 0.5-1.5 | Vegetation/grass lining | Vetiver grass, Kikuyu grass |
| 1.0-2.0 | Bio-engineering + light structural | Vetiver hedgerows + stone toe |
| 1.5-3.0 | Stone pitching or rip-rap | Locally quarried stone |
| 3.0-5.0 | Gabion baskets/mattresses | Wire mesh + local stone fill |
| 4.0-6.0 | Heavy gabions or concrete apron | Concrete or heavy gabions |
| > 6.0 | Energy dissipation structure | Specialist design required |

### M.2.2 Country-Specific Formulas

**Rip-rap D50 Sizing (Isbash method):**

```
D50 = V^2 / (2 * g * Cs * (Ss - 1))
```

Where V = flow velocity (m/s), g = 9.81 m/s2, Cs = stability coefficient (0.86 for high turbulence), Ss = specific gravity of stone (typically 2.5-2.7 for Kenya stone).

**Apron Dimensions:**

```
Apron length = 3 to 4 x culvert diameter (D) or channel width
Apron width = 2 x D + channel width (minimum 3 x D)
Apron thickness = 1.5 x D50, minimum 300 mm
```

**Gabion Mattress Sizing:**
- Mattress thickness: typically 0.3 m for moderate velocities, 0.5 m for higher velocities
- Standard basket length: 2.0-4.0 m
- Fill stone: 100-250 mm, hard, durable
- Wire: PVC-coated galvanized, minimum 2.7 mm diameter

**Extent of Protection at Channel Bends (RDM 2.2 Figure 6.1):**
Protection must extend a distance upstream and downstream of the bend apex as shown in Figure 6.1. Typically, protection extends from the point of curvature (PC) to beyond the point of tangency (PT).

### M.2.3 Spur/Groyne Design (RDM 2.2 Section 6.2.4)

Spurs redirect flow away from eroding banks:
- Spacing: typically 2-3 times the spur length
- Length: sufficient to redirect flow but not cause excessive constriction
- Angle: 60-90 degrees to flow direction (Figure 6.2 shows spur spacing and expansion angle)
- Materials: gabion baskets, stone masonry, or timber cribs

## M.3 Country-Specific Reasoning

### M.3.1 Decision Logic (Kenya Branch)

```
Step 4 (Kenya): Select protection type
  |-- V < 1.5 m/s and climate permits vegetation:
  |    Consider bio-engineering (Section 6.3.6)
  |    Vetiver grass hedgerows for slope protection
  |-- V = 1.5-3.0 m/s: stone pitching (Section 6.3.1/6.3.2)
  |    Kenya has good stone quarry availability in highlands
  |-- V = 3.0-5.0 m/s: gabion baskets/mattresses (Section 6.3.3)
  |    Gabion construction well-understood by Kenyan contractors
  |-- V = 4.0-6.0 m/s: heavy gabions or concrete apron
  |    Concrete more available in urban areas; gabions in rural
  +-- V > 6.0 m/s: ESCALATE -- energy dissipation structure
       Specialist hydraulic design per RDM 2.2 Ch 5 methods
```

### M.3.2 Professional Judgment (Kenya-Specific)

**Bio-engineering is explicitly promoted in RDM 2.2.** Section 6.3.6 provides guidance on using vegetation for erosion control. This is a significant addition compared to older manuals. Vetiver grass is widely available in Kenya and is effective for slope stabilisation. Bio-engineering should be considered as a first option for low-velocity situations (< 2 m/s) and as a complement to structural measures.

**Gabion construction is well-established in Kenya.** Kenya has experienced gabion contractors and readily available wire mesh. PVC-coated wire is essential for durability in Kenya's climate -- plain galvanized wire corrodes within 5-10 years.

**Stone availability varies by region.** The Kenya Highlands (Central, Western) have good quarry stone (basalt, granite). Coastal and arid regions may have limited stone availability -- gabions with smaller stone may be more practical than large rip-rap.

**ASAL regions present unique erosion challenges.** Flash floods in arid areas create intense erosion. Embankment protection in these areas must account for the high sediment and debris loads during infrequent but intense storms.

**River training is critical at bridge sites.** Section 6.2 provides specific guidance for protecting bridge approaches and abutments from river migration and scour. This links to Skill #39 (Bridge Hydraulics) for scour analysis.

### M.3.3 Common Errors (Kenya-Specific)

1. **Not specifying PVC-coated gabion wire.** Plain galvanized wire corrodes rapidly in Kenya's tropical climate. Always specify PVC-coated wire mesh.
2. **Ignoring bio-engineering options.** Section 6.3.6 promotes bio-engineering as a cost-effective, sustainable option. Too often, only structural solutions are considered.
3. **Omitting filter layers beneath stone pitching and gabions.** Lack of a filter layer leads to piping failure -- soil is washed out through the stone protection.
4. **Under-sizing protection extents.** Protection must extend sufficiently upstream and downstream of the vulnerable zone. For culvert outlets, a minimum of 3-4 culvert diameters downstream.
5. **Not accounting for future channel migration.** Rivers in Kenya's alluvial plains can migrate significantly. Protection design should account for potential future channel position.

## M.7 Worked Example -- Kenya

### Example: River Bank Erosion Protection for Road Embankment

**Source:** Based on RDM 2.2 Chapter 6 erosion control methods

**Given:**
- Road embankment adjacent to a seasonal river in Western Kenya
- Design flood velocity: V = 3.5 m/s
- Riverbed material: Sandy gravel
- Permissible velocity for sandy gravel: 1.5 m/s
- Embankment height: 4.0 m
- River bank slope: 2:1 (H:V)
- Local stone available: basalt from quarry 20 km away, sizes up to 400 mm

**Solution:**

**Step 1 -- Assess erosion risk:**
V_actual = 3.5 m/s >> V_permissible = 1.5 m/s (sandy gravel)
Erosion risk: HIGH -- protection required

**Step 2 -- Select protection type (Section 6.3):**
V = 3.5 m/s falls in the 3.0-5.0 m/s range --> Gabion baskets or heavy rip-rap

Options evaluation:
- Rip-rap: D50 = 3.5^2 / (2 * 9.81 * 0.86 * 1.6) = 12.25 / 27.0 = 454 mm -- locally available (up to 400 mm quarry stone, marginal)
- Gabion mattresses: use smaller stone (100-200 mm) in wire baskets -- readily available
- Bio-engineering: V = 3.5 m/s too high for vegetation alone -- but can supplement structural measures above the high water line

**Decision:** Gabion mattress revetment for bank face below HWL, with bio-engineering (vetiver grass) above HWL.

**Step 3 -- Design gabion revetment:**

Below high water level:
- Gabion mattresses: 2.0 x 1.0 x 0.5 m on bank slope
- Fill stone: 100-200 mm hard basalt, hand-packed
- Wire mesh: PVC-coated galvanized, 80 x 100 mm hexagonal
- Geotextile filter beneath mattresses (200 g/m2 non-woven)
- Toe protection: gabion basket (2.0 x 1.0 x 1.0 m) embedded 0.5 m below bed level

Above high water level:
- Vetiver grass hedgerows at 1.5 m vertical intervals on slope
- Topsoil and seed cover between hedgerows
- Section 6.3.6 bio-engineering approach

**Step 4 -- Determine extent of protection:**
- Upstream: extend 10 m upstream of the embankment toe (where flow impinges on bank)
- Downstream: extend 15 m downstream of the critical zone
- Vertical: from 0.5 m below riverbed to 0.5 m above design high water level

**Result:**

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Erosion risk | High (3.5 >> 1.5 m/s) | Protection required |
| Protection type | Gabion mattress + bio-engineering | Combined approach per Sections 6.3.3 and 6.3.6 |
| Gabion mattress | 2.0 x 1.0 x 0.5 m | PVC-coated wire, 100-200 mm stone fill |
| Toe protection | 2.0 x 1.0 x 1.0 m gabion | Embedded 0.5 m below bed |
| Filter | Geotextile, 200 g/m2 | Non-woven, beneath gabions |
| Bio-engineering | Vetiver grass hedgerows | Above HWL on embankment slope |
| Extent | 10 m upstream, 15 m downstream | Full bank height coverage |
