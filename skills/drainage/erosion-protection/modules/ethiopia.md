### M.1.2 Institutional Context

ERA's institutional framework for erosion protection:

- **ERA (Ethiopian Roads Authority):** Federal trunk roads (DC5-DC8). Full erosion protection design per DDM.
- **Regional Roads Authorities:** Regional roads (DC3-DC4). Same DDM standards apply; stone masonry and gabion solutions common.
- **Woreda Administrations:** Local access roads (DC1-DC2). Simplified protection measures; stone pitching and vegetation most common.

All agencies follow the ERA DDM for erosion protection design. Material availability drives the choice of protection method -- basalt and limestone are widely available throughout the Ethiopian Highlands, and gabion wire mesh is manufactured locally.

> Cross-reference: See Skill #13 Ethiopia module (M.1.2) for full institutional context. See Skill #28 Ethiopia module (material suitability) for rock quality requirements for erosion protection stone.

### M.1.3 Standard Philosophy

ERA DDM erosion protection follows a velocity-based approach: outlet velocity from culverts (Skill #31) or channel velocity from ditches (Skill #30) determines the type and extent of protection required. The manual provides methods for riprap sizing, gabion specification, energy dissipator selection, and check dam design. ERA places particular emphasis on locally available materials -- stone masonry, gabion baskets, and vegetation/bioengineering -- reflecting Ethiopian construction practice.

### M.2.2 Country-Specific Formulas

**Riprap D50 Sizing (Velocity-Based, ERA DDM / FHWA approach):**

```
D50 = K * V^2 / (2g * (Ss - 1))
```

Where D50 = median stone diameter (m), K = stability coefficient (typically 0.86 for uniform flow, 1.0-1.2 for turbulent outlet flow), V = design velocity (m/s), g = 9.81 m/s2, Ss = specific gravity of rock (default 2.65; Ethiopian basalt 2.7-2.9).

**Simplified riprap sizing (ERA practice):**

```
D50 = 0.040 * V^2.5 (for Ss = 2.65)
```

Where D50 is in metres and V in m/s. Adjust for Ethiopian basalt (Ss = 2.8):

```
D50 = 0.040 * V^2.5 * (2.65 - 1) / (Ss - 1) = 0.040 * V^2.5 * 1.65 / 1.80 = 0.037 * V^2.5
```

**Riprap Layer Thickness:**

```
t = max(1.5 * D50, D100)
```

**Apron Length for Culvert Outlet (ERA DDM):**

```
La = n * D (from Table E.32.5, where n = 3-5 depending on velocity)
```

**Scour Depth at Outlet (for plunge pool design):**

```
Ys = 0.42 * Q^0.5 * TW^(-0.25)
```

Where Ys = scour depth below streambed (m), Q = design discharge (m3/s), TW = tailwater depth (m). This is an empirical approximation; detailed scour analysis may be needed for large structures.

**Check Dam Spacing (ERA DDM):**

```
S_checkdam = h / (So - Sc)
```

Where S_checkdam = spacing between check dams (m), h = check dam effective height (m), So = natural channel slope (m/m), Sc = target reduced slope (m/m) that produces acceptable velocity.

### M.2.3 Material Selection for Ethiopian Conditions

**Basalt** is the most widely available rock type in the Ethiopian Highlands (covering much of the central plateau). Properties: specific gravity 2.7-2.9, very hard and durable, dark grey to black. Excellent for riprap, gabion fill, and stone masonry. The high specific gravity provides better erosion resistance than assumed in standard FHWA equations (which use Ss = 2.65).

**Limestone** is available in many parts of Ethiopia (northern highlands, parts of Rift Valley margins). Properties: specific gravity 2.5-2.7, moderately hard, susceptible to chemical weathering in acidic environments. Acceptable for riprap and gabion fill where not subject to acid water conditions.

**Gabion wire mesh** is manufactured locally in Ethiopia. Both galvanized and PVC-coated options are available. PVC-coated wire is recommended for permanent installations in aggressive environments (pH < 6 or > 9, or high sulfate soils).

### M.3.2 Professional Judgment (Ethiopia-Specific)

**Stone masonry is the dominant erosion protection method on Ethiopian roads.** While other East African countries rely primarily on loose riprap and gabion, Ethiopia has a strong stone masonry tradition. Mortared stone masonry provides excellent erosion protection (up to 3.0 m/s) and is cost-competitive with riprap in areas where skilled masons are available. The designer should consider stone masonry as the first option for moderate-velocity protection (1.5-3.0 m/s).

**Gabion availability has improved significantly in Ethiopia.** Local gabion wire mesh manufacture means gabion solutions are increasingly cost-effective. For flexible erosion protection on unstable ground or in areas subject to settlement, gabion mattresses and baskets are preferred over rigid stone masonry.

**Highland erosion is a national challenge.** The Ethiopian Highlands experience severe soil erosion due to steep terrain, heavy rainfall, and deforestation. Road construction can exacerbate erosion if drainage outfalls are not properly protected. The designer should provide erosion protection at every point where concentrated road drainage discharges to the natural terrain -- not just at major culvert outlets.

**Vetiver grass is widely used in Ethiopia for bioengineering erosion protection.** Vetiver hedgerows can be combined with structural measures (riprap, gabion) for long-term sustainable protection. Vetiver is drought-resistant and establishes well in Ethiopian conditions. Consider Vetiver grass strips at ditch outlets and on embankment slopes as a supplement to structural protection.

### M.3.3 Common Errors (Ethiopia-Specific)

1. **Omitting erosion protection at ditch outlets.** Every point where a side ditch discharges to the natural terrain needs protection. This is a common omission in Ethiopian road designs.
2. **Using limestone riprap in acidic soil conditions.** Limestone is susceptible to dissolution in acidic environments. Use basalt where soil pH < 6.
3. **Undersizing riprap because of basalt's higher specific gravity.** While basalt (Ss = 2.8) performs better than standard assumptions (Ss = 2.65), the improvement is only about 10% in D50. Do not reduce stone sizes significantly based on specific gravity alone.
4. **Not providing filter layers beneath riprap.** On fine-grained soils (silt, clay), riprap without a filter layer fails by piping -- fine soil particles wash through the riprap voids. Always specify a filter (geotextile or graded gravel).
5. **Specifying concrete energy dissipators where simpler solutions suffice.** For velocities < 4.5 m/s, riprap basins and gabion aprons are adequate and more maintainable than concrete stilling basins.

## M.7 Worked Example -- Ethiopia

### Example: Riprap Apron Design for Culvert Outlet on DC3 Regional Road

**Source:** Based on ERA DDM 2013 erosion protection methods with Ethiopian materials

**Given:**
- Culvert: 900 mm RCP under DC3 regional road
- Outlet velocity: V = 3.2 m/s (from Skill #31 Ethiopia module)
- Design discharge: Q = 2.5 m3/s (from Skill #29 Ethiopia module)
- Tailwater depth: TW = 0.3 m
- Downstream channel: Earth, laterite soil
- Available stone: Basalt (Ss = 2.8, LA Abrasion < 30%)

**Solution:**

**Step 1 -- Classify protection requirement:**
V = 3.2 m/s falls in the 3.0-4.5 m/s range (Table E.32.1). Recommended: heavy riprap or gabion mattress.

**Step 2 -- Size riprap (velocity-based):**

Using simplified formula adjusted for basalt:
```
D50 = 0.037 * V^2.5 = 0.037 * 3.2^2.5 = 0.037 * 18.38 = 0.680 m
```

This exceeds the Table E.32.2 riprap range. Using the table directly for V = 3.0-3.5 m/s: D50 = 300-400 mm.

The discrepancy is because the simplified formula is conservative. Use Table E.32.2 value: **D50 = 350 mm** (interpolated for V = 3.2 m/s).

D100 = 2.0 * D50 = 700 mm
Layer thickness = max(1.5 * 350, 700) = max(525, 700) = 700 mm

**Step 3 -- Size apron dimensions (Table E.32.5):**

V = 3.2 m/s falls in the 2.5-3.5 m/s range:
```
Apron length = 4D = 4 * 0.9 = 3.6 m
Apron width at downstream end = 4D + 0.6 m each side = 3.6 + 1.2 = 4.8 m
```

Apron transitions from culvert width (0.9 m) to 4.8 m at the downstream end, providing flow dissipation through expansion.

**Step 4 -- Specify filter layer:**

Downstream soil is laterite (fine-grained component). Provide:
- Non-woven geotextile filter fabric beneath riprap
- Or graded gravel filter: D15_filter / D85_soil < 5

**Step 5 -- Toe protection:**

Embed apron toe 300 mm below streambed level to prevent undermining.

**Step 6 -- Verify stone quality:**

Basalt rock source: Ss = 2.8, LA Abrasion < 30%, soundness loss < 12%. Acceptable per ERA material specifications.

> Cross-reference: Verify rock source quality against material suitability requirements per Skill #28 Ethiopia module.

**Step 7 -- Validation:**
- D50 = 350 mm is adequate for V = 3.2 m/s (within Table E.32.2 range)
- Apron length 3.6 m (4D) provides adequate energy dissipation for this velocity
- Basalt stone Ss = 2.8 > 2.65 assumed -- conservative design
- Layer thickness 700 mm provides full coverage

**Result:**

| Parameter | Value | Status |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Protection type | Heavy riprap apron | |
| Riprap D50 | 350 mm | Adequate for V = 3.2 m/s |
| Riprap D100 | 700 mm | |
| Layer thickness | 700 mm | >= D100 -- OK |
| Apron length | 3.6 m (4D) | Per Table E.32.5 |
| Apron width (downstream) | 4.8 m (4D + 1.2 m) | Flared for dissipation |
| Toe embedment | 300 mm below streambed | |
| Filter | Geotextile beneath riprap | |
| Stone source | Basalt, Ss = 2.8 | Per Skill #28 quality requirements |

Riprap apron: 3.6 m long, 350 mm D50 basalt riprap, 700 mm thick, on geotextile filter, with 300 mm toe embedment. Apron flares from 0.9 m to 4.8 m width.
