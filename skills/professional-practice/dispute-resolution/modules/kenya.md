### M.1.2 Institutional Context

**Kenya Road Authorities:** KeNHA (Kenya National Highways Authority) manages national and international trunk roads (Class A, B, C). KeRRA (Kenya Rural Roads Authority) manages rural and small town roads (Class D, E). KURA (Kenya Urban Roads Authority) manages urban roads. County Governments manage county roads. The Ministry of Roads and Transport (MoRT) provides policy oversight.

**Kenya Dispute Resolution Infrastructure:** Kenya has a well-developed dispute resolution infrastructure for construction disputes:

- **Chartered Institute of Arbitrators (CIArb) Kenya Chapter:** One of the largest CIArb chapters globally. Active in construction dispute resolution. Maintains a panel of accredited arbitrators and adjudicators with construction expertise. Provides training and professional development in dispute resolution.

- **Nairobi Centre for International Arbitration (NCIA):** Established by the NCIA Act 2013. Provides institutional arbitration services with modern rules and facilities. Increasingly specified in government construction contracts as the preferred arbitration institution. Has rules tailored to Kenya's legal framework.

- **Kenya Judiciary -- Construction and Arbitration Division:** The High Court of Kenya handles enforcement of arbitral awards and court-supervised dispute resolution. The Arbitration Act 1995 limits court intervention in arbitral proceedings (consistent with UNCITRAL Model Law principles).

**DAB/DAAB Practice in Kenya:** DABs are commonly used on large road contracts in Kenya, particularly donor-funded projects. Standing DABs (regular site visits) are preferred for large contracts. The FIDIC 2017 DAAB (Dispute Avoidance/Adjudication Board) concept is being adopted in some newer contracts.

## M.2 Country-Specific Knowledge

### M.2.1 Kenya Dispute Resolution Mechanisms

The Kenya dispute resolution framework for FIDIC construction contracts follows a graduated approach:

**Tier 1: Engineer's Determination (Sub-Clause 3.5 / 3.7)**
- The Engineer makes a fair determination after consulting both Parties.
- Under 1999 Red Book: Sub-Clause 3.5 (consult, endeavour to agree, fair determination).
- Under 2017 Red Book: Sub-Clause 3.7 (agreement or determination procedure).
- In Kenya practice, the Engineer's determination is the first resolution attempt.

**Tier 2: DAB/DAAB (Sub-Clause 20.2-20.4)**
- For contracts with a DAB: formal referral under Sub-Clause 20.4.
- DAB gives decision within 84 days.
- Decision is binding on both Parties -- must be complied with immediately.
- 28-day notice of dissatisfaction period (critical deadline -- if missed, decision becomes final and binding).
- Standing DABs provide proactive dispute avoidance through regular site visits.

**Tier 3: Mediation**
- Kenya Mediation Act 2012 provides a statutory framework for mediation.
- Some COPA include mediation as an additional step between DAB and arbitration.
- CIArb Kenya provides mediation services with experienced construction mediators.
- Mediation is voluntary (unless contractually required) but increasingly used.

**Tier 4: Arbitration (Sub-Clause 20.6)**
- Following the 56-day amicable settlement period (Sub-Clause 20.5).
- Options for arbitration institution:
  - NCIA (Nairobi Centre for International Arbitration) -- increasingly common for Kenya government contracts
  - CIArb Kenya -- established and respected
  - ICC (International Chamber of Commerce) -- FIDIC default
  - UNCITRAL Rules (ad hoc) -- some contracts specify this
- Seat of arbitration: typically Nairobi (per COPA).
- Kenya Arbitration Act 1995 provides the legal framework, based on UNCITRAL Model Law.
- Arbitral awards are enforceable through the High Court.

**Tier 5: Court Proceedings (Last Resort)**
- The High Court of Kenya has jurisdiction over construction disputes.
- However, where an arbitration agreement exists, the court will generally refer parties to arbitration.
- Court proceedings are typically used for: enforcement of arbitral awards, interim injunctive relief, challenges to arbitral awards on limited grounds (per Arbitration Act).

### M.2.2 COPA Modifications for Dispute Resolution

**Common modifications in Kenya road contracts:**
- Seat of arbitration: Nairobi (instead of ICC default)
- Arbitration institution: NCIA or CIArb Kenya (instead of ICC)
- Number of arbitrators: 3 for large contracts, 1 (sole) for smaller contracts
- Language of arbitration: English
- DAB/DAAB requirement: specified for contracts above a threshold value -- **ILLUSTRATIVE; verify COPA**
- Some contracts add a mediation step between DAB and arbitration

**Donor-funded project considerations:**
- World Bank and AfDB conditions may add dispute resolution requirements
- Some donor contracts require that fraud and corruption allegations are handled through separate donor procedures
- The MDB Harmonised Edition modifies some dispute resolution provisions

## M.3 Country-Specific Reasoning

### M.3.1 Decision Logic (Country Branch)

**Is a DAB/DAAB required for this contract?**
```
Check COPA for DAB/DAAB requirement:
  |-- COPA requires DAB/DAAB --> Appoint within 28 days of LOA
  |-- COPA silent on DAB --> FIDIC default applies (DAB required)
  +-- COPA removes DAB --> Direct resolution route:
                           Engineer determination -> amicable settlement -> arbitration
```

**Select appropriate arbitration institution:**
```
What do the COPA specify?
  |-- NCIA --> Use NCIA rules and facilities
  |-- CIArb Kenya --> Use CIArb Kenya rules
  |-- ICC --> Use ICC Rules (FIDIC default)
  |-- UNCITRAL --> Ad hoc arbitration under UNCITRAL Rules
  +-- Silent --> FIDIC default (ICC) applies unless parties agree otherwise
```

### M.3.2 Professional Judgment (Country-Specific)

**Kenya has strong arbitration infrastructure.** Unlike some jurisdictions in the region, Kenya has well-established arbitration institutions (NCIA, CIArb Kenya Chapter), experienced arbitrators, and a supportive legal framework (Arbitration Act 1995). This makes arbitration a realistic and effective dispute resolution mechanism.

**Encourage DAB/DAAB appointment early.** As in all jurisdictions, many contracts fail to appoint the DAB within the contractual deadline. The Engineer should remind both Parties of this obligation. A functioning DAB/DAAB reduces the likelihood of disputes escalating to arbitration.

**Mediation should be considered before arbitration.** Kenya's Mediation Act 2012 and the availability of experienced construction mediators through CIArb Kenya make mediation an attractive option. Mediation preserves commercial relationships and is significantly less costly than arbitration.

**Government disputes have additional complexities.** KeNHA, KeRRA, and KURA, as government agencies, may face constraints in settling disputes (budget authority, Auditor-General scrutiny, political considerations). Settlement authority may need to be obtained from the agency's Board or the National Treasury.

### M.3.3 Common Errors (Country-Specific)

1. **Not appointing the DAB/DAAB at contract commencement.** The 28-day deadline is frequently missed.
2. **Treating the DAB decision as advisory rather than binding.** DAB decisions are binding and must be complied with immediately.
3. **Ignoring the amicable settlement period.** Arbitration cannot commence until the 56-day period has elapsed (Sub-Clause 20.5).
4. **Assuming court proceedings are the primary route.** Where an arbitration agreement exists, the court will refer parties to arbitration per the Arbitration Act.
5. **Not considering mediation.** Mediation is often faster, cheaper, and less adversarial than arbitration.

- **Check:** Arbitration seat and institution specified in COPA
- **Pass condition:** Seat and institution specified (typically Nairobi / NCIA or CIArb Kenya)
- **Fail action:** NOTE -- Verify COPA provisions. FIDIC default (ICC) applies if silent.

- **Check:** 28-day notice of dissatisfaction given after DAB decision
- **Pass condition:** Notice given within 28 days of DAB decision
- **Fail action:** CRITICAL -- If deadline missed, DAB decision becomes final and binding. Cannot be challenged.

## M.7 Worked Example -- Kenya

### Example: KeNHA Road Project -- Interim Payment Dispute Escalation

**Project:** KeNHA trunk road construction, KES 8 billion, AfDB funded. FIDIC Red Book 1999. Standing 3-member DAB appointed.

**Scenario:** The Engineer certified IPC No. 10 at KES 320 million, but the Contractor claimed KES 410 million. The difference relates to the valuation of a rock excavation variation -- the Engineer valued rock at KES 4,800/m3 and the Contractor claims KES 6,200/m3. The Engineer's determination was issued on 15 February. The Contractor has expressed dissatisfaction and wishes to escalate.

**Solution:**

Step 1: Clarify procedure
- The Contractor is dissatisfied with the Engineer's determination under Sub-Clause 3.5.
- Next step: formal referral to the standing DAB under Sub-Clause 20.4.

Step 2: DAB referral (Sub-Clause 20.4)
- Contractor submits formal referral to the DAB including:
  - Statement of the dispute (rock excavation rate difference)
  - Engineer's determination and basis (KES 4,800/m3)
  - Contractor's claimed rate with supporting cost data (KES 6,200/m3)
  - Relevant site records, market data, and correspondence

Step 3: DAB process
- Standing DAB reviews submissions. May request additional information.
- DAB may visit site to inspect rock conditions and excavation methods.
- DAB gives decision within 84 days (by approximately 10 May).

Step 4: DAB decision
- DAB determines a rate of KES 5,400/m3 with detailed reasoning based on actual costs, market rates, and site conditions.
- Both Parties must comply immediately -- payment adjustment included in next IPC.

Step 5: If dissatisfied
- Either Party has 28 days from DAB decision to give notice of dissatisfaction.
- If no notice: decision becomes final and binding.
- If notice given: 56-day amicable settlement period (Sub-Clause 20.5).
- If not settled: arbitration under Sub-Clause 20.6.

Step 6: Arbitration (if reached)
- Per COPA: seat of arbitration is Nairobi.
- Institution: NCIA (as specified in COPA).
- Three arbitrators appointed per NCIA rules.
- Kenya Arbitration Act 1995 provides the legal framework.

Step 7: Practical considerations
- Dispute value: (KES 6,200 - 4,800) x estimated quantity = potential multi-million KES difference.
- Consider whether arbitration costs are proportionate. Mediation through CIArb Kenya may be more cost-effective.
- For AfDB-funded project: inform KeNHA to advise AfDB of the dispute and its status.
