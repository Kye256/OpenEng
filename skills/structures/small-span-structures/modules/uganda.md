### M.1.2 Institutional Context

MoWT Vol 4 provides bridge design principles that apply generally to small span structures but does not include a dedicated chapter on causeways, drifts, or submersible bridges. These structure types are widely used in Uganda on community access roads and district roads, designed according to engineering practice and local experience. UNRA and district engineers select small span structures based on traffic volume, flood regime, and available budget.

### M.1.3 Standard Philosophy

Uganda uses small span structures extensively due to the large number of low-volume community access and district roads (over 50,000 km of unclassified roads). Many seasonal rivers can be crossed with causeways or drifts at a fraction of the cost of a conventional bridge. The design approach is pragmatic: provide year-round access for as many days as economically justifiable, accepting that wet-season closure of a few days to a few weeks is the norm on low-class roads.

MoWT Vol 2, Section 9.6 states: "On low-cost low traffic roads no freeboard is required. The carriage way should be aligned to follow existing ground profile and cross dry watercourse at bed level. Flow will be allowed to pass over the road which may therefore become temporarily impassable to traffic during floods."

## M.3 Country-Specific Reasoning

### M.3.1 Decision Logic (Country Branch)

At Step 2 (flow regime assessment): Uganda has distinct wet and dry seasons. Many rivers in the northern and eastern regions are seasonal -- completely dry for 3-6 months. In these areas, causeways are practical and widely used. Central and western Uganda has more perennial rivers where drifts or low-level crossings are preferred.

At Step 4 (type selection): Apply Table USS.1 with local knowledge. Consider:
- **Availability of materials:** Stone pitching for causeways requires good quality stone nearby. Gabion baskets (Skill #32) are widely available in Uganda and commonly used for erosion protection at causeways.
- **Local construction capacity:** Causeways and drifts can be built with semi-skilled labour. Low-level crossings and submersible bridges require skilled labour and formwork.
- **Maintenance capacity:** Drifts require regular pipe clearing. If local maintenance capacity is limited, consider a plain causeway instead.

### M.3.2 Professional Judgment (Country-Specific)

**Seasonal rivers make causeways practical.** In Uganda's semi-arid regions (Karamoja, northern Uganda), seasonal rivers are completely dry for months. A causeway provides excellent service during the dry season at minimal cost.

**Gabion protection is standard in Uganda.** At causeway downstream edges, gabion mattresses or gabion baskets are the standard protection method. This connects to Skill #32 (Erosion Protection). Gabions use locally available stone and semi-skilled labour.

**Community roads are the primary market for small span structures.** Uganda has thousands of community access road river crossings that are currently unimproved fords. Upgrading these to concrete causeways or drifts provides major improvement at low cost.

**Flood warning infrastructure is often absent.** On community roads, formal flood depth markers and warning signs may not be installed due to cost and vandalism. At minimum, painted depth markers on the causeway approach posts should be specified.

### M.3.3 Common Errors (Country-Specific)

1. **Building causeways on perennial rivers.** If the river always has flow, a plain causeway will always be impassable. Build a drift (with pipes) or low-level crossing instead.
2. **Undersizing drift pipes.** In Uganda, pipe blockage by debris and sediment is common. Minimum pipe size should be 450 mm (not 300 mm) and regular clearing must be programmed.
3. **Not specifying scour protection at causeway downstream edge.** Overtopping flow accelerates at the downstream edge and causes severe scour. Gabion protection or stone pitching is essential.

### M.5.2 Standards Compliance Checks

- **Check:** Scour protection specified at downstream edge of causeway
- **Standard:** Engineering practice
- **Pass condition:** Gabion, riprap, or stone pitching specified
- **Fail action:** Flag -- add scour protection to prevent downstream erosion

- **Check:** Flood depth markers specified
- **Standard:** Road safety practice
- **Pass condition:** Depth markers or painted posts specified
- **Fail action:** Flag -- add flood warning signage

### M.5.3 Departures / Relaxations Process

No formal departures process for small span structures in Uganda. Engineering justification should be documented for any departure from the recommended type selection (e.g., building a causeway on a road with AADT > 150).

## M.7 Worked Example -- Uganda

### Example: Community Access Road -- Seasonal River Crossing

**Source:** Practice-based design guidance

**Given:**
- Community access road, AADT = 80 vehicles/day
- Seasonal river: normal flow Q_normal = 0.5 m3/s (during wet season base flow), dry for 5 months/year
- Q2 = 15 m3/s (2-year flood), Q10 = 45 m3/s
- Acceptable closure: 10 days/year
- River bed width: 12 m
- Budget: limited (community road)

**Solution:**

**Step 1: Type selection**
- AADT = 80 --> suitable for causeway or drift (Table USS.1)
- Flow regime: seasonal with wet-season base flow of 0.5 m3/s
- Since base flow exists during wet season, a plain causeway would be overtopped even outside major floods. Recommend **vented causeway (drift)** to pass the base flow.
- 10 days/year closure acceptable for community road (Table USS.4)

**Step 2: Pipe sizing for drift**
- Normal flow: 0.5 m3/s
- From Table USS.3: 2-3 pipes of 600 mm diameter
- Check: Q_pipe = 3 x 0.7 x (pi/4 x 0.6^2) x (2 x 9.81 x 0.4)^0.5
  = 3 x 0.7 x 0.283 x 2.80 = 1.66 m3/s (assuming 0.4 m head)
- 3 x 600 mm pipes pass up to approximately 1.7 m3/s --> handles base flow with margin

**Step 3: Slab design**
- Causeway slab: 250 mm thick reinforced concrete
- Width: match road width (6 m for community road) + shoulders
- Length: 12 m (river bed width) + 3 m approaches each side = 18 m total

**Step 4: Scour protection**
- Downstream edge: gabion mattress, 300 mm thick, extending 3 m downstream
- Side protection: stone pitching 150 mm thick

**Step 5: Flood warning**
- Painted depth markers at both approaches (0.1 m intervals to 0.5 m)
- Warning sign: "FLOOD CROSSING -- DO NOT ENTER WHEN FLOODED"
- Reflective guide posts along approaches

**Step 6: Closure assessment**
- Structure overtops when Q > Q_pipes (approximately 1.7 m3/s)
- Based on flow frequency analysis, Q > 1.7 m3/s for approximately 30-40 days/year during wet season
- However, trafficable depth (< 300 mm) may be available for some overtopping events
- Effective closure approximately 10-15 days/year for depths > 300 mm
- Acceptable for community road

**Result:**
- Structure type: Vented causeway (drift)
- Pipes: 3 x 600 mm diameter concrete pipes
- Slab: 250 mm RC, 18 m long, 6 m wide
- Scour protection: gabion mattress downstream
- Estimated closure: 10-15 days/year (within acceptable range)
- Flood warning: depth markers and warning signs
