### M.1.2 Institutional Context

In the UK, causeways and drifts are rarely used on public roads. Traffic volumes on virtually all public roads exceed the thresholds where overtopping structures are acceptable. UK safety standards and driver expectations do not tolerate planned flood closures on public highways.

### M.1.3 Standard Philosophy

The UK does not have specific design standards for causeways, drifts, or submersible bridges on public roads. DMRB provides standards for all highway structures, and these generally assume zero planned closures due to flooding. Ford crossings exist on some very minor rural roads (typically byways open to all traffic or unclassified roads) but are legacy features rather than new design choices.

## M.3 Country-Specific Reasoning

### M.3.1 Decision Logic (Country Branch)

In the UK context, the parent skill's type selection logic (Section 2.1) leads almost always to "conventional bridge" because:
- UK public road AADT is generally well above 500, even for minor roads
- UK safety standards and public expectation require all-weather access
- Flood closure of public roads requires formal road closure procedures and diversions
- Legal liability for drowning at flooded crossings is a significant concern

### M.3.2 Professional Judgment (Country-Specific)

**Fords exist in the UK but are legacy features.** There are several hundred ford crossings on minor roads in England and Wales, mostly on byways and unclassified roads. These are not new designs -- they have existed for decades or centuries. Many have depth gauges and warning signs.

**UK fords have safety concerns.** Vehicle rescue at flooded fords is a regular occurrence in the UK during winter floods. Several drowning deaths have occurred at UK fords. There is growing pressure to replace fords with bridges or culverts.

**Private roads and farm access may use small span structures.** On private roads (farm tracks, estate roads, forestry roads), causeways and fords are still used. These are outside the public highway design standards.

### M.3.3 Common Errors (Country-Specific)

1. **Applying UK standards to developing country contexts.** UK standards assume high traffic volumes and zero-tolerance for flood closure. These assumptions do not apply in developing countries where causeways and drifts are standard and appropriate.

## M.7 Worked Example -- UK

### Example: UK Context for Small Span Structures

**Source:** Knowledge-based

**Given:** Same scenario as Uganda module (AADT 80, seasonal river, Q2 = 15 m3/s)

**UK Assessment:**
In a UK context, this scenario would be unusual. An AADT of 80 is very low for a UK public road but still above the threshold where planned flood closure is acceptable. The recommended approach would be:

1. If the crossing is on a public highway: install a conventional bridge or large culvert. The economic case for a causeway does not hold in the UK due to higher labour costs, liability concerns, and public expectation of all-weather access.
2. If the crossing is on a private road (farm access, forestry): a ford crossing with depth gauge and warning sign may be acceptable. The landowner accepts the flood closure risk.
3. DMRB does not provide specific guidance for submersible structures. Any new structure on a public highway would be designed to DMRB bridge standards.

**Key difference from Uganda:** In Uganda, the cost differential between a causeway (UGX 20-50 million) and a conventional bridge (UGX 500 million - 2 billion) makes causeways economically rational for community roads. In the UK, the cost differential is smaller relative to total project costs, and the liability/safety costs of flood closures outweigh the construction savings.
