### M.1.2 Institutional Context

**MoWT Vol 7 (Road Safety):** Provides the national framework for road safety auditing in Uganda. Includes guidance on RSA procedures, team composition, and audit stages. MoWT Vol 7 is the primary reference for RSA on all Uganda road projects.

**UNRA:** Requires RSA for all national road projects. For donor-funded projects (World Bank, AfDB), RSA at detailed design stage is typically mandatory as a condition of the financing agreement.

**National Road Safety Council (NRSC):** Uganda's coordinating body for road safety policy. Provides policy direction but does not directly conduct RSAs.

**Police Traffic and Road Safety Directorate:** Maintains crash records, though data quality and completeness vary. Crash data for existing road audits may be incomplete -- supplement with site inspection and community consultation.

### M.1.3 Standard Philosophy

MoWT Vol 7 adopts a pragmatic approach to RSA, recognising the East African road environment. The standard emphasises:
- Independence of audit team from design team
- Systematic checklist-based approach
- Consideration of all road users, especially vulnerable road users (pedestrians, cyclists, boda-bodas)
- Practical, implementable recommendations
- Recognition that many safety issues stem from the absence of infrastructure (no footpaths, no lighting) rather than design deficiencies

## M.2 Country-Specific Knowledge

### M.2.1 Uganda RSA Requirements

**Mandatory RSA stages for UNRA projects:**

| Funding Source | RSA Requirement | Stage |
|---------------|----------------|-------|
| GoU budget | RSA recommended per MoWT Vol 7 | Detailed design + pre-opening |
| World Bank | RSA mandatory per financing agreement | Detailed design (minimum) |
| AfDB | RSA mandatory per financing agreement | Detailed design (minimum) |
| Any source | RSA recommended for high-accident sections | Existing road inspection |

**Audit team composition (Uganda):**

MoWT Vol 7 recommends:
- Team leader: Registered engineer with RSA training or 10+ years road design experience
- Road safety specialist: Person with formal RSA training (iRAP, TRL, or equivalent)
- Minimum team size: 2 for design-stage audits, 3 for pre-opening and existing road audits
- Additional members for large projects: traffic engineer, NMT specialist, environmental specialist

### M.2.2 Uganda-Specific Hazard Inventory

**Top road safety hazards on Uganda roads (UNRA network):**

1. **Pedestrians on carriageway (rural):** Most national roads outside urban areas have no footpaths. Pedestrians -- including children walking to school -- walk on the carriageway or paved shoulder.
2. **Boda-boda hazards:** Uganda has an estimated 500,000+ boda-bodas. They operate on all road types, frequently carry multiple passengers, rarely use helmets (despite legal requirement), and create conflict points at junctions and trading centres.
3. **Speed at trading centres:** Trading centres along national roads experience speeds designed for rural driving (80-100 km/h) conflicting with pedestrian activity. Speed management (signs, humps, rumble strips) is often absent or ineffective.
4. **Heavy vehicles on steep gradients:** On mountain roads (particularly Rwenzori, Mt Elgon approaches), overloaded trucks lose control on steep downgrades. Runaway truck ramps are rare.
5. **Night-time visibility:** Limited street lighting outside urban areas. Many vehicles (especially boda-bodas and bicycles) have no functioning lights. Pedestrians in dark clothing are invisible to drivers.
6. **Open drainage ditches:** Deep, steep-sided drainage ditches adjacent to the carriageway are a hazard to vehicles leaving the road and to pedestrians.
7. **Animal crossings:** Cattle, goats, and other livestock cross roads in rural areas, particularly in northern and eastern Uganda (pastoralist regions).

### M.2.3 Speed Management at Trading Centres

The transition from rural to trading centre is the most critical safety zone on Uganda national roads. MoWT and UNRA use the following speed management measures:

| Measure | Typical Application | Effectiveness |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|
| Speed limit signs (50 km/h) | At entry to trading centre | Low if not reinforced |
| Speed humps (flat-top) | 50-100m before first building | High, but uncomfortable for trucks |
| Rumble strips | Advance warning, 200-300m before centre | Moderate -- audible/tactile warning |
| Raised pedestrian crossings | At main crossing points within centre | High -- combines crossing with speed reduction |
| Gateway treatment | Visual narrowing at entry point | Moderate -- creates perception of speed change |

## M.3 Country-Specific Reasoning

### M.3.1 Decision Logic (Country Branch)

**Uganda-Specific Audit Process:**
```
Step 2 (Uganda): Check RSA requirements
  |-- UNRA project --> RSA per MoWT Vol 7 at minimum; check financing agreement
  |-- World Bank funded --> RSA mandatory at detailed design stage (minimum)
  |-- AfDB funded --> RSA mandatory at detailed design stage (minimum)
  +-- District road --> RSA recommended but not always required

Step 3 (Uganda): Augment checklist with East African hazards
  --> Add: pedestrians on carriageway assessment
  --> Add: boda-boda conflict assessment (junctions, trading centres)
  --> Add: animal crossing assessment (if rural/pastoralist area)
  --> Add: trading centre speed transition assessment
  --> Add: night-time NMT visibility assessment
```

### M.3.2 Professional Judgment (Country-Specific)

**Trading centre safety is the number one priority for most Uganda RSAs.** The speed transition from rural (80+ km/h) to trading centre (should be 30-50 km/h) is where the most severe pedestrian crashes occur. Auditors must assess: (a) advance warning signs, (b) speed reduction measures, (c) pedestrian crossing facilities, (d) sight distance through the centre, and (e) boda-boda stage locations relative to the carriageway.

**Absence of facilities is a finding, not a "no comment."** When a road design does not include footpaths through a trading centre, the auditor must flag this as a Serious Concern, not simply note "NMT: not included in design." The omission of pedestrian facilities in a pedestrian-intensive environment is a safety risk.

**Community knowledge supplements crash data.** Uganda crash data (from Police) is incomplete. Community leaders, health centres, and school teachers can provide information about crash locations, near-misses, and high-risk periods. This supplements the formal crash record.

### M.3.3 Common Errors (Country-Specific)

1. **Using AUSTROADS RSA checklist without Uganda-specific additions.** AUSTROADS checklists are designed for Australian conditions (separated facilities, low NMT, no boda-bodas). Uganda audits require local hazard items.
2. **Not conducting night-time inspection.** Many Uganda road safety hazards (unlit pedestrians, unlit boda-bodas, missing delineation) are only visible at night. A daytime-only audit misses critical risks.
3. **Recommending expensive solutions without low-cost alternatives.** Recommending "construct 10km of footpath" (high cost, long implementation) without also recommending "install warning signs and painted edge markings" (low cost, immediate implementation) fails to address the immediate risk.
4. **Ignoring boda-boda stages.** Boda-boda stages (waiting areas) at junctions and trading centres create conflict points. Auditors must check whether stages are located away from the carriageway and junctions.

### M.5.2 Standards Compliance Checks

- **Check:** East African-specific hazards included in checklist
- **Standard:** MoWT Vol 7 / iRAP East Africa
- **Pass condition:** Boda-boda, pedestrian-on-carriageway, and speed transition items are assessed
- **Fail action:** NOTE -- East African hazard items not assessed. Add boda-boda, pedestrian, and speed transition items.

- **Check:** Night-time inspection conducted (pre-opening/existing)
- **Standard:** MoWT Vol 7
- **Pass condition:** Night-time inspection documented
- **Fail action:** SENIOR REVIEW -- Night-time inspection not conducted. Critical for assessing delineation and NMT visibility.

### M.5.3 Departures / Relaxations Process

MoWT Vol 7 does not have a formal departures process for RSA. However, the design team must formally respond to all Serious Concerns and Recommendations, documenting whether each is accepted (and how it will be implemented) or rejected (with written justification).

## M.6 Country-Specific Escalation

| Trigger | Reason | Suggested Action |
|---------|--------|-----------------|
| No pedestrian facilities in trading centre | Major safety gap | SENIOR REVIEW -- No footpaths or pedestrian facilities through trading centre with high pedestrian activity. This is a Serious Concern. |
| No speed management at trading centre | High pedestrian crash risk | SENIOR REVIEW -- No speed reduction measures (humps, signs, narrowing) at transition to trading centre. Recommend gateway treatment. |
| No crash data available | Cannot assess crash patterns | NOTE -- No crash data from Police records. Supplement with community consultation and site inspection. |
| Boda-boda stages on carriageway | Conflict point | RECOMMENDATION -- Boda-boda stage located on carriageway/junction. Relocate to designated off-road waiting area. |

## M.7 Worked Examples -- Uganda

### Example 1: Detailed Design RSA for Road Through Trading Centre

**Project:** Detailed design stage RSA for 15 km paved road (UNRA project, World Bank-funded) passing through Nyamitanga Trading Centre (km 6-8).

**Given:**
- Road: 7.0m carriageway + 1.5m paved shoulders (both sides)
- Design speed: 80 km/h (rural sections), 50 km/h posted through trading centre
- Trading centre: 2 km long, high pedestrian activity, market day twice per week
- Two T-junctions within trading centre
- No footpaths designed through trading centre
- Two boda-boda stages at the junctions
- AADT: 800 vpd, 35% boda-bodas

**Audit Findings:**

SC-1 (Cross-section): No pedestrian facilities through Nyamitanga Trading Centre.
- Road users affected: Pedestrians (including school children)
- Risk: Pedestrians forced to walk on carriageway alongside traffic at 50+ km/h
- Mitigation: Construct minimum 1.5m footpath on both sides through trading centre (2 km). Raised pedestrian crossings at junctions. (Reference: Skill #20, NMT Facilities)
- Cost: High

SC-2 (Speed management): No physical speed reduction measures at trading centre entry.
- Risk: Speed limit sign alone insufficient -- vehicles likely to enter at 60-80 km/h
- Mitigation: Install flat-top speed humps at km 5.8 and km 8.2 (both entries), with advance rumble strips at km 5.5 and km 8.5. Gateway treatment (visual narrowing) at entry points.
- Cost: Medium

R-1 (Intersections): Boda-boda stages at T-junctions obstruct sight triangle.
- Mitigation: Relocate boda-boda stages minimum 30m from junction. Designate off-carriageway waiting area with hardstanding.
- Cost: Low

R-2 (Drainage): Open drainage ditch (0.8m deep, steep-sided) adjacent to carriageway through trading centre.
- Risk: Hazard to pedestrians and vehicles leaving carriageway
- Mitigation: Cover ditch through trading centre with concrete slabs or install kerbed drainage. (Reference: Skill #30)
- Cost: Medium

O-1 (Signs): Speed limit signs (50 km/h) provided at entry, but no repeater signs within trading centre.
- Mitigation: Install repeater signs at km 7.0 (mid-point of trading centre).
- Cost: Low

**Result:** 2 Serious Concerns, 2 Recommendations, 1 Observation. Design must address SC-1 and SC-2 before proceeding to construction.

### Example 2: Existing Road Safety Inspection of High-Accident Section

**Project:** Existing road safety inspection of 8 km section of paved national road in eastern Uganda with reported high accident rate.

**Given:**
- Road: Paved, 6.5m carriageway, no shoulders (worn away)
- AADT: 1,200 vpd
- Crash data: 12 reported crashes in last 2 years (5 involving pedestrians, 4 involving boda-bodas, 3 head-on)
- Section includes 2 sharp curves (R < 200m) and 1 trading centre

**Key Findings:**

SC-1: Sharp curve at km 3.2 (R = 150m) has no advance warning sign and vegetation obstructs sight distance.
SC-2: Trading centre at km 5.0 has no speed management measures. 5 of 12 crashes occurred within 500m of the trading centre.
R-1: Shoulders eroded to earth -- pedestrians walk on carriageway edge on a 1,200 vpd road.
R-2: Boda-boda stages at both curves obstruct forward visibility.

**Recommendation:** Prioritise low-cost measures (signs, markings, vegetation clearance, speed humps) for immediate implementation. Programme shoulder rehabilitation and pedestrian facilities for next maintenance/rehabilitation contract.

### Example 3: Audit with No NMT Facilities in Peri-Urban Area

**Project:** Detailed design RSA for 12 km road rehabilitation in peri-urban area outside Jinja. Road passes through 3 trading centres and 2 school zones. High foot traffic observed (estimated 2,000+ pedestrians/day). No pedestrian facilities included in the design.

**Given:**
- Design: 7.0m carriageway + 1.0m gravel shoulders
- Design speed: 60 km/h
- No footpaths, no pedestrian crossings, no school zone treatments
- Budget: UGX 18 billion (constrained -- client has stated NMT facilities are "separate budget")

**Audit Findings:**

SC-1 (Critical): Complete absence of NMT facilities on a road carrying 2,000+ pedestrians/day through 3 trading centres and 2 school zones. This is the most significant safety gap in the design.

**Escalation:** SENIOR REVIEW -- The absence of NMT facilities on this road represents a foreseeable and severe safety risk. The auditor recommends that the design cannot be considered complete without NMT provisions. At minimum:
- Raised pedestrian crossings at 5 locations (2 schools, 3 trading centres) -- Low cost
- Speed management at all trading centres (speed humps, signs) -- Low/Medium cost
- Painted edge markings to define pedestrian walking space on shoulders -- Low cost
- Footpath construction through the 3 trading centres (approximately 3 km total) -- flagged as HIGH cost, to be included in project scope or separate budget

If the full footpath budget is genuinely unavailable:
- Prioritise the low-cost measures (crossings, speed management, markings) as immediate minimum
- Document the footpath requirement as a separate budget item with clear justification
- The auditor must record that the Serious Concern remains unresolved for the footpath component
