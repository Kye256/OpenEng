### M.1.2 Institutional Context

**KeNHA (Kenya National Highways Authority):** Client for RSA on national trunk roads (Class A/B/C). KeNHA commissions RSA through the project Engineer. RSA is increasingly required for all KeNHA road projects, particularly donor-funded ones.

**KeRRA (Kenya Rural Roads Authority):** Client for RSA on rural roads (Class D/E). RSA is less systematically applied on rural roads but is required for major improvement projects, particularly donor-funded.

**KURA (Kenya Urban Roads Authority):** Client for RSA on urban roads. Urban RSA has additional considerations including pedestrian facilities, public transport stops, and cycling infrastructure.

**County Governments:** May commission RSA on county roads for major projects. RSA capacity at county level is limited.

**NTSA (National Transport and Safety Authority):** Kenya's road safety regulator. NTSA collects crash data, regulates driver licensing, and enforces vehicle safety standards. NTSA data informs RSA background information (crash history at project location). Kenya has among the highest road fatality rates in Africa -- an estimated 13 people are killed and 44 injured every day in road crashes (PAM-4 Section 2.2, citing Economic Survey 2023).

**Engineers Board of Kenya (EBK):** Registers professional engineers who may serve as RSA team members.

### M.1.3 Standard Philosophy

PAM-4 adopts a safe system approach to road safety audit. The audit is proactive -- it aims to prevent crashes by identifying safety hazards before they cause harm. The audit team is independent of the design team. PAM-4 provides stage-specific checklists (Appendix A, Tables A.1-A.5) and case studies (Appendix B) for Kenya context. RSA is NOT a compliance check with design standards -- it is a safety assessment from the road user's perspective.

## M.2 Country-Specific Knowledge

### M.2.1 RSA Stages (PAM-4 Section 2.4)

PAM-4 defines 5 RSA stages:

| Stage | Name | When Conducted | Key Focus |
|-------|------|---------------|-----------|
| 1 | Feasibility / Preliminary Design | After feasibility study completion | Design standards, route choice, alignment, cross-sections, interchange/intersection layouts |
| 2 | Detailed Design | After detailed design, before contract documents | Geometric layout, pavement markings, signals, road signs, intersection details, sight triangles, roadside objects, vulnerable road users |
| 3 | Construction | During construction | Traffic management, work zone safety, pedestrian safety, worker safety, signage, transition zones |
| 4 | Pre-Opening | Before opening to traffic | Installation of signs/markings/delineation, night-time visibility, safety for all road users |
| 5 | Post-Completion / Existing Road | After opening or for existing roads | Operational safety deficiencies, signage, guardrails, pedestrian facilities, accessibility |

### M.2.2 RSA Checklists (PAM-4 Appendix A)

PAM-4 provides comprehensive checklists for each stage:
- **Table A.1:** Stage 1 checklist (feasibility/preliminary design) -- covers design standards, road alignment, cross-section, intersections, NMT, drainage, adjacent land use
- **Table A.2:** Stage 2 checklist (detailed design) -- covers geometric details, sight distance, markings, signs, roadside hazards, pedestrians, cyclists, motorcycles
- **Table A.3:** Stage 3 checklist (construction) -- covers traffic management, work zones, pedestrian/cyclist provisions, temporary signage
- **Table A.4:** Stage 4 checklist (pre-opening) -- covers sign/marking installation, night visibility, delineation, safety barriers
- **Table A.5:** Stage 5 checklist (post-completion/existing) -- covers operational safety, markings, guardrails, pedestrian facilities, lighting

### M.2.3 Risk Assessment (PAM-4 Section 3.6)

**Severity and Likelihood Assessment (Table 3.1):**

| Likelihood | Severity: Fatal/Serious | Severity: Moderate | Severity: Minor |
|------------|------------------------|-----------------------|------------------|
| Frequent | Intolerable | Intolerable | Moderate |
| Probable | Intolerable | Significant | Moderate |
| Occasional | Significant | Moderate | Tolerable |
| Improbable | Moderate | Tolerable | Tolerable |

**Priority Levels for Recommendations (Table 3.3):**
- **Priority 1 (Immediate):** Intolerable risk -- requires immediate action
- **Priority 2 (Short-term):** Significant risk -- requires action within defined timeframe
- **Priority 3 (Medium-term):** Moderate risk -- should be addressed in normal programme
- **Priority 4 (Long-term):** Tolerable risk -- monitor and address if practical

### M.2.4 Kenya-Specific Safety Concerns

**Motorcycle (boda-boda) safety:** Kenya has over 2 million registered motorcycles. Motorcyclists account for a significant proportion of road fatalities (1,270 killed in 2021 per PAM-4). RSA must specifically assess: motorcycle lane provisions, junction safety for motorcycles, speed management in motorcycle-heavy areas, and motorcycle parking/staging areas near markets and transport hubs.

**Pedestrian safety:** 1,558 pedestrians killed in 2021 (PAM-4). Pedestrians are the most at-risk group. Urban and peri-urban areas require particular attention to: footpaths, crossing facilities, speed management near schools and markets, street lighting, and separation from vehicular traffic.

**Livestock on roads:** On rural roads, particularly in pastoral areas (North Eastern, parts of Rift Valley), livestock on the road is a significant hazard. RSA for rural roads should assess visibility, speed management, and warning signage for livestock crossing areas.

**Speed management near schools and markets:** Kenya has significant roadside trading and school zones along rural and peri-urban roads. Speed transition zones require clear signage, road markings, and physical speed management (see Skill #70 Kenya module -- speed humps per RDM 6.4).

**Matatu (public transport) stops:** Informal and formal matatu stops create pedestrian-vehicle conflict points. RSA should assess lay-by design, pedestrian crossing facilities near stops, and sight distance at matatu stopping areas.

### M.2.5 Audit Team Requirements (PAM-4 Section 3.1)

- Team of 2-3 members minimum (PAM-4 recommends 2-3 for most projects)
- Team Leader: Senior Road Safety Auditor with adequate qualification and experience
- Team competencies: traffic engineering, road design and construction, crash investigation, road user behaviour
- Team must be independent of the design team
- Auditor should have attended approved RSA training programme and be a certified auditor
- For minor/low-volume road projects: 1 Senior RSA Auditor + 1 Trainee Auditor acceptable

## M.3 Country-Specific Reasoning

### M.3.1 Decision Logic (Country Branch)

**Kenya-specific branching:**
```
Determine RSA stage and road context:
  |-- Stage 1 (Feasibility/Preliminary Design):
  |     Use PAM-4 Table A.1 checklist
  |     Focus: standards, route choice, cross-section, intersections
  |
  |-- Stage 2 (Detailed Design):
  |     Use PAM-4 Table A.2 checklist
  |     Focus: geometry, markings, signs, sight triangles, VRU provisions
  |     Kenya-specific: motorcycle lanes, pedestrian crossings, matatu stops
  |
  |-- Stage 3 (Construction):
  |     Use PAM-4 Table A.3 checklist
  |     Focus: work zone safety, TMP, pedestrian/cyclist provisions
  |
  |-- Stage 4 (Pre-Opening):
  |     Use PAM-4 Table A.4 checklist
  |     Night inspection essential (sign reflectivity, delineation, lighting)
  |
  +-- Stage 5 (Post-Completion/Existing):
        Use PAM-4 Table A.5 checklist
        Focus: operational deficiencies, pedestrian facilities, guardrails
        Kenya-specific: boda-boda staging areas, school zones, market areas
```

### M.3.2 Professional Judgment (Country-Specific)

**Kenya's road fatality rate is high.** 4,579 killed in 2021, with cost of fatalities estimated at 9.1% of GDP (World Bank). This creates strong policy impetus for systematic RSA. Auditors should be aware of the scale of the safety challenge.

**The safe system approach requires consideration of all road users.** In the Kenya context, this explicitly includes: pedestrians (including persons with disabilities), cyclists, motorcycle riders, matatu passengers boarding/alighting, and livestock handlers. Vulnerable road users are disproportionately affected.

**RSA findings are recommendations, not instructions.** Per PAM-4, the auditor identifies safety concerns and recommends mitigation. The client (road authority) decides which recommendations to implement. The auditor does NOT redesign the project. All changes are made by the designer, not the auditor.

### M.3.3 Common Errors (Country-Specific)

1. **Treating RSA as a compliance check.** RSA is NOT a check against design standards -- it is a safety assessment from the road user's perspective. Compliance with standards does not guarantee safe design.
2. **Not inspecting at night.** PAM-4 specifically requires day AND night inspection for Stages 4 and 5. Many safety deficiencies (sign visibility, delineation, lighting) are only apparent at night.
3. **Ignoring motorcycle safety.** Given the scale of motorcycle traffic in Kenya, any RSA that does not address motorcycle safety is incomplete.
4. **Audit team not independent of design team.** PAM-4 Section 3.1 requires independence. Team members should not have prior experience working on the same project.

### M.5.2 Standards Compliance Checks

- **Check:** Appropriate stage checklist used
- **Standard:** PAM-4 Appendix A
- **Pass condition:** Checklist matches project stage (Table A.1-A.5)
- **Fail action:** NOTE -- Verify checklist matches RSA stage. Using wrong checklist may miss stage-specific safety concerns.

- **Check:** Audit team independence
- **Standard:** PAM-4 Section 3.1
- **Pass condition:** Audit team members are independent of the design/construction team
- **Fail action:** SENIOR REVIEW -- Audit team independence compromised. RSA validity requires independence per PAM-4.

### M.5.3 Departures / Relaxations Process

RSA methodology follows PAM-4. There is no formal departures process for RSA itself -- the audit methodology is standardised. However, the client may choose not to implement specific audit recommendations, which must be documented with reasons in the Corrective Action Report (CAR).

## M.7 Worked Examples -- Kenya

### Example 1: Preliminary Design RSA for Class B Road

**Project:** Stage 2 (Detailed Design) road safety audit for a Kenya Class B road preliminary design through a small town.

**Given:**
- Class B road, 80 km/h design speed reducing to 50 km/h through town
- Design includes: 50 km/h speed zone, 2 pedestrian crossings, 3 side road junctions
- Town population: 15,000
- Significant motorcycle traffic (estimated 30% of ADT)
- School located 100m from road alignment
- Market area with roadside trading

**Solution:**

Step 1: Apply PAM-4 Table A.2 (Stage 2 checklist):

Step 2: Key audit findings:

**Finding 1 (Priority 1 -- Intolerable):** Speed transition from 80 to 50 km/h not adequately managed. No advance warning signs shown on drawings. No physical speed reduction measures (speed humps, gateway treatment). Risk: vehicles entering town at 80 km/h with pedestrians present.
- Recommendation: provide advance warning signage per RDM 6.2, gateway treatment with physical speed management per RDM 6.4, road markings per RDM 6.1.

**Finding 2 (Priority 1 -- Intolerable):** School zone 100m from road with no dedicated crossing facility. No school zone signage shown. No speed reduction to 30 km/h near school.
- Recommendation: provide school zone signage, dedicated pedestrian crossing with raised platform, 30 km/h speed zone during school hours, footpath connecting school to crossing.

**Finding 3 (Priority 2 -- Significant):** Motorcycle traffic at 30% but no motorcycle lane provisions assessed. Junction design does not show motorcycle waiting areas.
- Recommendation: assess motorcycle lane warrants per RDM 1.3 Section 4.7. Provide motorcycle waiting areas at junctions.

**Finding 4 (Priority 2 -- Significant):** Market area with roadside trading. No separation between trading area and carriageway. No lay-by for matatu stops.
- Recommendation: provide trading area setback or barrier, matatu lay-by with pedestrian crossing, clear sight lines at market area.

**Result:** Stage 2 RSA identifies 4 safety concerns (2 Intolerable, 2 Significant) requiring design modifications before contract documents. Key issues: speed management through town, school zone safety, motorcycle provisions, market area pedestrian safety. Corrective Action Report (CAR) prepared per PAM-4 Section 3.9 for client response.
